## Section 3 — HSSC Editorial Preference Inference

### What HSSC Editors Appear to Reward
1. **Explicit interdisciplinary contribution statement** — papers state in abstract/introduction which two or more disciplines they bridge
2. **Policy or societal implication paragraph** — most published articles have a dedicated ~200-word "policy implications" or "societal relevance" section in Discussion
3. **Framework papers are welcome** — HSSC regularly publishes papers whose primary contribution is a new theoretical construct with "illustrative" or "demonstration" empirics (e.g., The Existential Hologram paper)
4. **Cross-species / evolutionary framing is neutral to positive** — treated as methodological breadth, not scope violation
5. **Quantitative rigour is not penalised** — large-N, meta-analytic, MR-based papers appear regularly

### Desk-Reject Triggers (Inferred)
1. **Obvious recycled-submission fingerprints** — manuscript metadata, version-tracking text, or references to a different journal's format conventions still visible
2. **Human-only scope with no disciplinary bridge** — pure psychology lab study with no social/policy implications
3. **Breadth without any depth signal** — too many layers with no flagship finding stated in abstract
4. **Missing humanities/social science framing** — paper reads entirely as biology/medicine with no bridge to social science domains

### Abstract Opening Style
- HSSC published abstracts typically open with a **problem statement** or **phenomenon** (not a rhetorical question)
- Social/policy framing in sentence 1 is common (e.g., "Smartphone addiction has emerged as a public health concern…")
- Cross-species or evolutionary framing can appear but is typically sentence 2–3, after the human-level phenomenon is established

### Cover Letter Style
- HSSC cover letters should lead with **disciplinary bridge** ("This paper contributes to both evolutionary behavioural science and social policy…")
- Do **not** reproduce NHB's "Honest Limitations" paragraph structure — HSSC editors do not expect authors to proactively enumerate study weaknesses in the cover letter
- APC waiver requests, if applicable, should be stated directly

### Recommendation A (Preferred): "Cross-species construct + policy/humanities wings" (2–3 days work)
**Title adjustment:**
- Current (NHB): "Sweet Trap: a theory of reward–fitness decoupling with cross-species empirical validation"
- HSSC option: **"Sweet Trap: a cross-disciplinary framework for reward–fitness decoupling and its implications for behavioural policy"**
- Rationale: Adds "cross-disciplinary framework" (HSSC keyword) and "behavioural policy" (humanities/social-policy bridge); retains the construct name and scientific content.

**Abstract first sentence rewrite:**
- Current: "Moths pursue artificial lights; sea turtles eat plastic; humans aspirationally over-consume investment leverage, short-video, mortgages, alcohol."
- HSSC rewrite: "Across human societies, the same pattern recurs: individuals aspirationally pursue goals — financial leverage, digital media, addictive substances — that deliver high perceived reward while eroding long-term fitness. Moths navigating artificial lights and sea turtles ingesting plastic demonstrate this reward–fitness decoupling across species."
- Rationale: Opens with human-society phenomenon (HSSC framing); cross-species evidence positioned as supporting evidence rather than lead, without removing it.

**Cover letter pitch (HSSC version):**
> We submit "Sweet Trap" as a contribution bridging evolutionary behavioural science, behavioural economics, and public health policy. The paper formalises a unifying axiomatic construct — reward–fitness decoupling — that explains why signal-redesign interventions (sugar taxes, algorithmic transparency mandates, fraud-prevention architecture) structurally outperform information campaigns across six human domains, with the asymmetry bound derived as a theorem (not an empirical generalisation) and confirmed empirically. Validation spans 25 ISSP countries, 19 Mendelian-randomisation chains, and 3,000 pre-registered specifications. We believe this combination of formal theory, cross-disciplinary empirics, and a directly policy-relevant theorem aligns well with HSSC's scope at the intersection of social sciences and humanities.
