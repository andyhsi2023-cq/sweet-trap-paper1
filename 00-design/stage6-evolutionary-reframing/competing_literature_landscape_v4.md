## Critical Finding: No Prior Paper Has Made This Claim

**No published paper simultaneously claims: (1) cross-phyla universality of reward-fitness decoupling + (2) evolutionary formation of the mechanism + (3) formal model + (4) human-animal unified framework.**

The two closest competitors divide the claim in half:
- Robertson & Chalfoun 2016: half of it (evolutionary trap, animal-only, no model, no humans)
- Li & van Vugt 2018: the other half (mismatch, human-only, no model, no animals)

Your paper is the synthesis that neither has attempted. There is no "you're dead" competitor.

## Section 1: Ecological Trap / Evolutionary Trap — Top Reviews + Empirical (2011-2026)

### [ET-1] Robertson, B.A., Rehage, J.S., & Sih, A. (2013)
Ecological novelty and the emergence of evolutionary traps.
*Trends in Ecology & Evolution*, 28(9), 552-560. DOI: 10.1016/j.tree.2013.04.004

Two-sentence abstract: HIREC (human-induced rapid environmental change) elevates ecological traps into evolutionary traps when novel environments emerge faster than natural selection can respond; provides a classification of trap types by permanence and mechanism. Framework rests on attractiveness-fitness mismatch in habitat selection without a formal mechanistic model.

Overlap with our claim: Direct conceptual predecessor — Ecological Trap ⊂ MST ⊂ Sweet Trap. Robertson et al. establish the evolutionary timescale framing we build on.
Our novel delta: (1) We add formal endorsement condition A3.0 (agents epistemically lack access to decoupling); (2) we add the self-reinforcing equilibrium ρ (Robertson et al. describe traps as static mismatches, not escalating ones); (3) we extend to human behaviour; (4) we add the EST (Engineered) subclass.
v3.3 citation status: CITED (ref 6 in main).

### [ET-2] Robertson, B.A., & Chalfoun, A.D. (2016)
Evolutionary traps as keys to understanding behavioral maladaptation.
*Current Opinion in Behavioral Sciences*, 12, 12-17. DOI: 10.1016/j.cobeha.2016.08.007

Two-sentence abstract: Extends evolutionary trap concept to explain persistent behavioral maladaptation in wildlife; argues that evolutionary traps are underappreciated as drivers of population decline and extinction risk. ### [ET-7] Bauer, C.M., & Sih, A. (2020)
Evolutionary traps in changing environments.
*Current Biology*, 30(23), R1430-R1435. [DOI to verify in publication]

Two-sentence abstract: Reviews current state of evolutionary trap research, identifying what prevents evolutionary escape from traps as the central unresolved question; discusses the role of behavioural plasticity, rapid learning, and evolutionary rescue. Emphasises that the trap field lacks a predictive framework for when traps are permanent vs transient.

Overlap: Directly states the open question that Sweet Trap addresses at the comparative-biology level — why can't species escape? We answer this with (a) the within-phylum receptor-architecture conservation tier (H4a), which constrains the space of rapid behavioural reconfiguration, (b) the cross-phylum convergent-architecture finding (H4b), which shows that reward-system solutions are repeatedly selected toward the same functional design and therefore carry similar escape constraints across independent origins, and (c) the lineage-specific positive-selection signatures (H6a), which show that when species do escape (or adapt within) Sweet Trap environments, the escape is marked by detectable molecular footprints.
Our novel delta: Bauer & Sih describe the problem at a review level without a quantitative cross-phylum test; our paper provides the first operational Δ_ST measure + phylogenetic-signal test + convergent-architecture diagnostic + lineage-specific positive-selection scan, all pre-registered, on this open question.
v3.3 citation status: Listed in literature_cross_species.md as [VERIFY] — confirm DOI and add to main reference list. Should be cited in Introduction.

## Section 2: Supernormal Stimulus + Sensory Trap

### [SS-1] Ryan, M.J., & Cummings, M.E. (2013)
Perceptual biases and mate choice.
*Annual Review of Ecology, Evolution, and Systematics*, 44, 437-459. DOI: 10.1146/annurev-ecolsys-110512-135901

Two-sentence abstract: Reviews how pre-existing sensory biases (tuning of perceptual systems for non-signalling purposes) are exploited by signal evolution, creating "sensory traps" where receivers respond to stimuli based on ancient perceptual preferences rather than current fitness value. Covers cases from fish to frogs to insects to humans, establishing sensory exploitation as a cross-phyla phenomenon.

Overlap: Sensory trap is equivalent to Sweet Trap Route B (supernormal stimulus). Ryan & Cummings provide the most systematic review of the cross-species sensory exploitation literature.
Our novel delta: Ryan & Cummings focus on signal-receiver coevolution (sexual selection context); they do not model the self-reinforcing equilibrium, do not consider human behaviour, and do not develop the endorsement concept.
v3.3 citation status: NOT CITED. MUST-CITE for Route B (supernormal stimulus) mechanism. Reviewers in behavioural ecology will expect this.

### [SS-2] Tinbergen, N. (1951)
The Study of Instinct. Oxford University Press.

Historical origin of the supernormal stimulus concept (oystercatcher preferring giant artificial eggs over own eggs; herring gull chick begging preferring more contrasting dummy beak). The founding empirical demonstration that evolved response systems can be "hijacked" by exaggerated stimuli.
v3.3 citation status: NOT CITED. Should appear in Introduction or SI as the original supernormal stimulus case. Tinbergen 1951 establishes the concept's 70-year history — not citing it looks like a gap.

## Section 3: Reward Mismatch / Evolutionary Mismatch

### [RM-1] Li, N.P., & van Vugt, M. (2018)
The evolutionary mismatch hypothesis: implications for psychological science.
*Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 27(1), 38-44. DOI: 10.1177/0963721417731378

Two-sentence abstract: Systematic review of evolutionary mismatch hypothesis across multiple psychological domains (food preference, social comparison, risk assessment, learning biases, emotional regulation); proposes that mismatch degree is quantifiable via the gap between ancestral and current environmental demands. Argues for integrating evolutionary mismatch into mainstream psychological research.

Overlap: Multi-domain + evolutionary origin + quantification aspiration — this is the most direct conceptual competitor in the "mismatch as general principle" space. Li & van Vugt make a similar universality claim for humans.
Our novel delta vs Li & van Vugt: (1) They are human-only; we include 50+ non-human species across ≥ 6 phyla. (2) They have no operational measure; we define Δ_ST as a signed, cross-species-comparable scalar with axioms A1-A4 and a Price-equation-derived prediction on phylogenetic signal K > 0.30. (3) They have no endorsement (F2) condition — they treat mismatch as passive suffering, while Sweet Trap requires the agent to actively choose and maintain the mismatched stimulus, which is what enables cross-species comparison. (4) They have no phylogenetic-signal analysis. (5) They have no molecular-convergence diagnostic. (6) They have no lineage-specific positive-selection scan. (7) They have no cross-species genetic-manipulation literature synthesis under common coding.
v3.3 citation status: NOT CITED. MUST-DIFFERENTIATE. Any reviewer who knows this literature will ask "how is Sweet Trap different from Li & van Vugt's evolutionary mismatch hypothesis?" You must preemptively answer this in Introduction paragraph 3.

## Section 4: Drive / Meiotic Drive / Selfish Genetic Elements

### [SGE-1] Burt, A., & Trivers, R. (2006)
Genes in Conflict: The Biology of Selfish Genetic Elements. Harvard University Press.

Two-sentence summary: Systematic treatment of genetic elements (meiotic drive, transposons, genomic imprinting, cytoplasmic male sterility) that propagate by circumventing normal inheritance, often at cost to organismal fitness. Shows that within-genome conflict is a universal feature of sexually reproducing organisms.

Theoretical kinship with Sweet Trap: Selfish genetic elements "hijack" the transmission machinery (analogous to how Sweet Trap stimuli hijack reward machinery). The "fitness cost to the host organism" pattern is formally similar. However, the direction and mechanism are inverted: SGEs act at the molecular level, Sweet Traps act at the environmental-behavioural level.
Verdict: Theoretical analogy exists but is weak. Not a direct competitor. Including in Discussion as a "broader pattern of within-organism hijacking" is an option but risks diluting the focus. Recommend NOT citing in main text unless you have a specific cross-level theoretical point to make in Discussion.
v3.3 citation status: NOT CITED. Not required.
