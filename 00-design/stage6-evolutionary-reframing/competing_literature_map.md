## 1. Top Reviews and Synthesis Papers (last 10 years)

### 1.1 Robertson, Hutto and colleagues — Evolutionary Trap canonical strand

- **Robertson, Rehage & Sih (2013)** *Ecological novelty and the emergence of evolutionary traps.* Trends Ecol Evol 28:552.
- **Robertson & Hutto (2006)** *A framework for understanding ecological traps.* Ecology 87:1075.
- **Hale & Swearer (2016)** *Ecological traps: current evidence and future directions.* Proc R Soc B 283:20152647.

**Their contribution:** Establish the ecological-trap construct — animals preferring low-fitness habitats — and formalise the "perceptual mismatch + fitness consequence" two-condition definition. Hale & Swearer provide the largest systematic review (~60 cases) and classify by habitat type.

**Where they stop:**
- No formal scalar measure of decoupling magnitude (Δ_ST equivalent absent).
- No phylogenetic-signal analysis of susceptibility.
- Human scope limited to casual analogy; no behavioural or genetic human evidence.
- No cross-phylum molecular conservation content.

**Sweet Trap's delta:**
- Formal scalar (Δ_ST = U_perc − E[U_fit]) allows quantitative cross-case comparison.
- Phylogenetic-signal test (K, λ) answers "universal or convergent?" quantitatively.
- Humans integrated as one clade (Part 1), with trans-ancestry MR.
- Molecular-conservation evidence (Part 3) establishes the mechanistic basis.

**Strategic risk:** Any Proc B referee who knows Hale & Swearer 2016 will ask "how is this more than a taxonomic extension of that review?" The answer is in the method (phylogenetic signal + molecular conservation), not the case compilation.

### 1.2 Sih and HIREC (Human-Induced Rapid Environmental Change) strand

- **Sih, Ferrari & Harris (2011)** *Evolution and behavioural responses to HIREC.* Evol Appl 4:367.
- **Sih (2013)** *Understanding variation in behavioural responses to HIREC.* Anim Behav 85:1077.
- **Candolin & Wong eds. (2012)** *Behavioural Responses to a Changing World.* Oxford Univ Press.

**Their contribution:** Frame the ecological-trap phenomenon within the broader HIREC concept (climate change, pollution, urbanisation); emphasize behavioural flexibility as moderator.

**Where they stop:** No formal model of when flexibility rescues vs. locks in. No cross-phylum phylogenetic analysis. No integration with human behavioural genetics.

**Sweet Trap's delta:** Our F1+F2 conditions specifically separate *voluntary endorsement* (F2) from *coerced exposure* — the latter is HIREC-type mismatch without endorsement and is *not* a Sweet Trap. This distinction clarifies a confusion in the HIREC literature (e.g., animals that *must* pass through polluted corridors are not in Sweet Trap; animals that *prefer* polluted-cue habitats are).

### 1.3 Nesse evolutionary-mismatch strand

- **Nesse (2005)** *Maladaptation and natural selection.* Quart Rev Biol 80:62.
- **Nesse (2019)** *Good Reasons for Bad Feelings.* Dutton. [trade book]
- **Lea, Tennie, et al. (2018)** *Understanding individual differences in evolutionary mismatch.* Biol Letters 14:20180138.
- **Li, van Vugt & Colarelli (2018)** *The evolutionary mismatch hypothesis.* Curr Dir Psychol Sci 27:38.

**Their contribution:** The strongest theoretical lineage for a human Sweet Trap claim. Mismatch theory identifies modern environments as producing maladaptive psychological responses (obesity, anxiety, addiction).

**Where they stop:**
- Mismatch is diagnosed, not measured. No scalar for magnitude, no cross-species comparison.
- The *voluntary endorsement* feature (our F2) is not isolated — mismatch theory treats all maladaptive responses uniformly rather than separating coerced-exposure mismatch from endorsed-approach mismatch.
- Cross-phylum evidence mostly anecdotal.

**Sweet Trap's delta:** Operationalization. Mismatch + endorsement + measurable Δ_ST + phylogenetic structure.

**Critical missing engagement (and why we will fix it):** Nesse's work has been under-engaged in prior v3.x — in v3.4 the name-dropping was largely Veblen/Schor/Frank (social-theory), while the *actual* theoretical parent (Nesse) was only cited in passing. **We will move Nesse citations to a prominent Introduction position** and articulate the delta in the first three paragraphs.

### 1.4 Lefebvre and brain-behaviour ecology

- **Lefebvre, Reader & Sol (2004)** *Brains, innovations and evolution in birds and primates.* Brain Behav Evol 63:233.
- **Reader & Laland (2002)** *Social intelligence, innovation, and enhanced brain size in primates.* PNAS 99:4436.
- **Sol, Duncan, Blackburn, Cassey & Lefebvre (2005)** *Big brains, enhanced cognition, and response to novel environments.* Proc Natl Acad Sci USA.

**Their contribution:** Cross-species data linking brain size, behavioural innovation, and ecological success. A methodological template for phylogenetic-comparative analysis of behavioural traits.

**Relation to us:** Their paradigm is "larger brains → better flexibility → better fit in novel environments." Sweet Trap claims that *even when brains are large enough for flexibility, reward-system architecture produces trap dynamics*. So we are not contradicting Lefebvre — we are identifying a specific mechanism (reward-fitness decoupling under endorsement) that brain flexibility does not necessarily rescue. This is a complementary positioning.

**Citation strategy:** Cite as methodological template for PGLS analysis (H5) and as example of successful cross-species behavioural-evolution synthesis.

---

### 1.5 Bateson and supernormal-stimulus lineage

- **Bateson & Healy (2005)** *Comparative evaluation and its implications for mate choice.* Trends Ecol Evol 20:659.
- **Tinbergen (1948)** — historical foundation of supernormal stimulus (cited but not deeply engaged in v3.x).
- **Barrett (2010)** *Supernormal stimuli: How primal urges overran their evolutionary purpose.* [trade book]

**Their contribution:** Identify supernormal-stimulus as the specific mechanism by which engineered cues (advertising, pornography, ultra-processed food) exceed the ancestral stimuli they evolved to respond to.

**Where they stop:** No phylogenetic-comparative analysis; no molecular-conservation content; human work largely anecdotal.

**Sweet Trap's delta:** Sweet Trap *includes* supernormal-stimulus cases as one subclass (Route B in F1 taxonomy) and adds the endorsement + persistence + feedback-blocking conditions. This maps supernormal-stimulus onto the broader decoupling framework.

### 2.1 Competitive threat assessment

**Highest-risk prior art:** Hale & Swearer 2016 (case compilation) and Barrett & Lightfoot 2023 (recent review). These establish the field but *do not perform phylogenetic-signal analysis on decoupling magnitude*. Our contribution survives because:

1. No prior paper has operationalized decoupling as a continuous scalar and tested its phylogenetic distribution.
2. No prior paper has integrated ecological-trap + Fisher-runaway + supernormal-stimulus under one operational framework with a testable signature.
3. No prior paper has paired the animal meta with human trans-ancestry MR and cross-phylum molecular conservation in one analysis.

**Emerging competitive risk:** A well-funded lab (Sih group, Robertson group, or a European behavioural-ecology group) could produce a similar phylogenetic-signal paper in 6-12 months if they have infrastructure ready. Our timing advantage is the 12-week horizon — fast enough to priority-claim the phylogenetic-signal + molecular-conservation combination.

## 5. "Old Wine in New Bottles" Defence

**The risk:** A Proc B or NEE reviewer reads the paper and says *this is a re-packaged ecological-trap / evolutionary-mismatch story with a new name (Sweet Trap) and some fancy phylogenetic-signal numbers.*

**Our response:**

1. **Sweet Trap is an operational-measurement contribution, not a construct-replacement.** We retain "ecological trap" and "evolutionary mismatch" as field terms; Sweet Trap is the *measurement* that puts numbers to what those terms describe.
2. **Phylogenetic-signal analysis on decoupling magnitude is novel.** No prior paper has answered "how does susceptibility distribute across the tree of life?" with K/λ on Δ_ST.
3. **Trans-ancestry MR + cross-phylum molecular conservation combined is novel.** No prior paper has combined these two evidence streams at the comparative-biology level.
4. **The F1+F2 separation clarifies field confusion.** We formalise *voluntary endorsement* as distinct from *coerced exposure*, which the ecological-trap and HIREC literatures have conflated.

**How we word this in the Introduction:** *"The [evolutionary trap, ecological trap, evolutionary mismatch, supernormal stimulus, Fisher runaway, sensory exploitation] literatures have documented extensive cases of reward-fitness decoupling over the past three decades, yet the phenomenon lacks a common operational measurement and has never been subject to phylogenetic-comparative analysis at the Animalia scale. Here we introduce Sweet Trap as an operational framework [...]"*

This language is precise: it explicitly credits the prior literature and positions our contribution as operational, measurement-level, and comparative-biology-level — not as a new name for an old thing.
