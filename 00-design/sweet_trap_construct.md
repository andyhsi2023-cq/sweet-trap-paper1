## 0. Executive summary

The **Sweet Trap** is a pattern in which an individual, acting rationally under their information and incentives, **persistently endorses a choice that reduces their own long-run welfare**. It is *not* a bias, *not* a mistake, and *not* a failure of cognition. It is an **economic equilibrium** generated by the joint activation of four behavioral primitives:

1. **θ (amenity/pleasure weight)** — the chosen option delivers high first-order utility in period 1
2. **λ (externalization share)** — a share of the period-2 cost falls on cohorts other than the decision-maker
3. **β (hyperbolic present-bias)** — the decision-maker over-weights period-1 gains against period-2 costs
4. **ρ (status-quo lock-in)** — once engaged, preferences adapt toward the sweet option and reversal is costly

When {θ, λ, β, ρ} are all strictly positive, the individual's subjective optimum I* strictly exceeds the social optimum I**, and the wedge I* − I** is monotone increasing in each primitive. The decision-maker's continued endorsement of I* is observable via subjective well-being reports, revealed choice, and (at aggregate level) political ratification signals.

The Sweet Trap is distinct from all existing behavioral-economic constructs because it is **cross-domain by construction** and **equilibrium-based rather than bias-based**. Sunk cost is past-anchored; loss aversion operates at the decision moment; hyperbolic discounting is one temporal component; status-quo bias is one persistence component. None alone predicts that a rational agent will *actively endorse* a welfare-reducing choice across heterogeneous domains. The Sweet Trap does.

## 1. The formal construct

### 1.1 Definition (operational and falsifiable)

An agent *i* is in a Sweet Trap with respect to choice dimension *I* (investment, consumption, or engagement level) iff the following six conditions hold jointly:

- **D1 (Short-term well-being increase):** Increasing *I* at period *t₁* raises *i*'s reported momentary well-being *W_{i,1}* (within-person, holding fixed *i*-level time-invariant traits).
- **D2 (Long-term welfare reduction):** The same increase in *I* at *t₁* reduces *i*'s objectively measured welfare at period *t₂ > t₁* (consumption, health, wealth, mental health, or equivalent welfare metric), holding fixed the intervening environment.
- **D3 (Amenity positivity, θ > 0):** *i*'s period-1 utility has a strictly positive derivative in the amenity delivered by *I*.
- **D4 (Externalization, λ > 0):** A strictly positive share of the period-2 cost falls on cohorts other than *i* (spouse, children, future self, other residents, next generation, lenders).
- **D5 (Present-bias, β < 1):** *i*'s quasi-hyperbolic present-bias parameter is strictly less than 1 (Laibson 1997).
- **D6 (Lock-in, ρ > 0):** Once *I* is at level *I_t*, *i*'s marginal utility of an additional unit is strictly greater than their marginal disutility of a symmetric reduction — preferences exhibit asymmetric adjustment around the current level.

**Equilibrium condition.** Under D3–D6, the agent's subjective first-order condition delivers I*(θ, λ, β, ρ) > I**, where I** is the welfare-maximizing level for a social planner aggregating cohorts with equal weights and no present-bias.

**Observable signature.** The joint occurrence of (D1) positive within-person *well-being response* and (D2) negative within-person *welfare response* to the same movement in *I*, together with active endorsement of *I* (revealed preference, stated preference, or political ratification), constitutes the Sweet Trap signature.

### 1.2 What the construct is not

- Not a **bias** in the sense of a deviation from optimal decision-making. The agent is rational under their objective; the suboptimality is relative to a *social* welfare criterion.
- Not a **preference reversal**. The agent's preference ordering is stable; it is the cost allocation (via λ) and the temporal weighting (via β) that generate the wedge.
- Not a **cognitive failure**. The four primitives are features of the agent's preferences and environment, not misperceptions.
- Not **universal**. The construct predicts a trap *only* when all four primitives are active. If any of λ = 0, β = 1, or ρ = 0, the wedge collapses or attenuates. (Section 5 discusses boundary conditions.)

### 1.3 Formal statement

Consider a representative agent *i* with a two-period decision problem. Let *I* ≥ 0 denote the choice dimension, *A = f(I)* the amenity stock with *f' > 0, f'' < 0*, and *δ(I)* the period-2 welfare cost with *δ' > 0, δ'' > 0*. The agent's lifetime utility is:

$$
U_i = u(c_{i,1}, A_1, A_0) + \beta_i \gamma \, \mathbb{E}\bigl[(1-\lambda_i) \cdot u(c_{i,2}, A_2, A_1)\bigr]
$$

with period utility:

$$
u(c, A, A_{-1}) = \ln c + \theta \cdot g(A) + \rho \cdot h(A - A_{-1})
$$

where *g* is concave, *h* is convex in the first argument, and *c_{i,2} = \bar{Y}[1 - δ(I)] - (1+r)I*.

**Sweet Trap Proposition (generalized, domain-free).** Under the utility and technology structure above, the agent-optimal *I*(θ, λ, β, ρ)* strictly exceeds the social-planner first-best *I***, and

$$
\frac{\partial (I^* - I^{**})}{\partial \theta} > 0, \quad
\frac{\partial (I^* - I^{**})}{\partial \lambda} > 0, \quad
\frac{\partial (I^* - I^{**})}{\partial (1-\beta)} > 0, \quad
\frac{\partial (I^* - I^{**})}{\partial \rho} > 0.
$$

Proof: differentiate the two first-order conditions with respect to each primitive at the (λ=0, β=1, ρ=0) corner; concavity of *g, f* and convexity of *δ* deliver interior uniqueness. (Full algebra in SI §A.)

**One-sentence plain-English version.** *People endorse the choice that makes them worse off because the pleasure comes now, the pain comes later, and some of the pain will fall on somebody else.*

### 1.4 Falsifiability conditions (what kills the construct)

The Sweet Trap is falsified if *any* of the following is empirically established across multiple candidate domains:

- **F1:** Within-person well-being response to *I* is null or negative (kills D1 → no trap to explain).
- **F2:** Long-run welfare response to *I* is null or positive (kills D2 → the "trap" is actually a net benefit).
- **F3:** λ = 0 groups (high-commitment, no-externalization subsamples) exhibit the *same* endorsement as λ > 0 groups, with appropriate statistical power (kills the externalization pillar).
- **F4:** The four primitives fail to jointly activate in any domain with the required welfare asymmetry (kills cross-domain universality).
- **F5:** Endorsement reverses upon full-information provision (kills the "rational under their incentives" claim — the phenomenon becomes a cognitive failure, not an equilibrium).

Each candidate domain provides an independent falsification opportunity. Cross-domain *convergence* on D1–D6 is the evidentiary bar for the construct's existence.

## 2. Boundary with existing behavioral-economic constructs

The construct's novelty depends on it being distinct from — not merely a relabeling of — existing behavioral concepts. Below is a precise mapping. *Emphasis added to the key differentiator in each row.*

| Existing construct | Canonical source | Core assumption | Relation to Sweet Trap |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Sunk cost fallacy** | Arkes & Blumer 1985 | Past unrecoverable investment distorts current decisions | SC is **past-anchored**; ST is **forward-looking equilibrium**. An agent in a Sweet Trap has no historical commitment; they would make the same choice *de novo*. |
| **Loss aversion** | Kahneman & Tversky 1979 | Losses weighted ~2× more than equivalent gains | LA operates at the **decision moment** around a reference point; ST operates across **temporal periods** and **cohorts**. ST uses no reference point; it uses an intertemporal budget constraint. |
| **Hyperbolic discounting** | Laibson 1997; Frederick, Loewenstein & O'Donoghue 2002 | Discount rate between now and soon > discount rate between soon and later | HD is the **β component** of ST. HD alone predicts present-biased consumption; it does *not* predict that the agent will *endorse* a policy/choice that is suboptimal. Endorsement requires λ > 0 in addition. |
| **Status-quo bias** | Samuelson & Zeckhauser 1988 | Current state is privileged in choice | SQB is the **ρ component** of ST. SQB alone predicts inertia; it does *not* predict active *escalation* of the sweet choice over time. Escalation requires θ > 0 (fresh amenity) and β < 1 (present-bias) in addition. |
| **Endowment effect** | Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler 1991 | Goods owned are valued higher than identical goods not owned | EE is a **static manifestation** of ρ; ST is a **dynamic equilibrium** across two periods. |
| **Adaptive preferences** | Elster 1983; Sen 1999 | Preferences adjust to circumstance, especially under constraint | AP explains why disadvantaged agents downgrade aspirations; ST explains why agents *upgrade* engagement with a welfare-reducing option. AP is passive accommodation; ST is active endorsement under high θ. |
| **Choice architecture / nudge** | Thaler & Sunstein 2008 | External information environment shapes choices | CA is an **external intervention** aimed at correcting bias; ST is an **internal equilibrium** that persists *despite* full information. Nudges away from Sweet Traps run against a monotone-preference gradient, not merely against inattention. |
| **Default effects** | Madrian & Shea 2001 | Defaults drive outcomes through inattention | Defaults are **passive acceptance**; ST is **active endorsement** with non-trivial cognitive engagement. The Sweet Trap survives full disclosure, defaults do not. |
| **Temptation / self-control** | Gul & Pesendorfer 2001 | Agent has preferences over restricted choice sets to resist temptation | T/SC predicts the agent *wants* commitment devices. ST predicts the agent *rejects* commitment devices because the current choice is subjectively optimal under their (λ, β)-augmented preferences. |
| **Rational addiction** | Becker & Murphy 1988 | Forward-looking agent rationally consumes addictive goods | RA is the closest existing construct. **Key difference:** RA has *no externalization* (λ = 0); the agent internalizes all future costs. ST's signature is precisely that λ > 0. Also, RA is domain-specific (addiction); ST is domain-general. |
| **Behavioral public economics wedge** | Bernheim & Taubinsky 2018 | Individuals make choices that deviate from welfare-maximizing; policy should correct | BPE provides the *aggregate welfare framework* for Sweet Trap analysis. ST is a specific structural instance: the wedge decomposes into exactly four primitives, and one of them (λ) is inter-cohort rather than intra-personal. |
| **Narrow bracketing** | Read, Loewenstein & Rabin 1999 | Decisions evaluated in isolation rather than jointly | NB is a **cognitive-framing** explanation; ST is a **preference-and-incentive** explanation. NB can contribute to ST by making λ salient only at the individual margin, but ST does not require NB. |

**The critical differentiator.** Sweet Trap is an **economic equilibrium phenomenon**, not a cognitive bias. The agent is not making an error. They are optimizing, subject to primitives (θ, λ, β, ρ) that differ from those of a utilitarian social planner. This distinguishes it from every row above except *rational addiction*, from which ST is distinguished by λ > 0 (externalization) and domain generality.

**Most easily confused with.** Rational addiction. The disambiguating test: in a Sweet Trap, *non-addictive* domains (e.g., educational spending, bride price) exhibit the same wedge structure, and λ > 0 empirically. Rational addiction cannot accommodate either.

## 3. Cross-domain universality (6+ instantiations)

The Sweet Trap construct is meaningful only if it can be instantiated across structurally different domains without becoming a tautology. Below we specify eight candidate domains; each must satisfy D1–D6 with a substantively interpreted (θ, λ, β, ρ). Domains with conflicting mechanism specifications (e.g., if λ is identically zero) are *not* candidates.

### Domain-level (θ, λ, β, ρ) interpretation matrix

| # | Domain | Sweet choice *I* | θ (amenity/pleasure) | λ (externalization) | β (present-bias) | ρ (lock-in) |
|:---:|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **1** | **Urban over-investment** (anchor) | New infrastructure delivery | Public-amenity consumption (metro, park, CBD) raises period-1 life satisfaction | Share of period-2 debt burden falling on out-migrants, next generation, fiscal transfer recipients | Hyperbolic weighting of period-1 amenity vs period-2 debt | Consumption complementarity with new infrastructure; sunk visibility |
| **2** | **Intensive parenting / "鸡娃" education spending** | Extracurricular spend per child | Parental status/identity utility from child's apparent success | Child bears long-run mental-health cost; parent internalizes only observable achievement | Parent over-weights visible period-1 markers (grades, tutoring) | Peer-referenced spending floors; difficulty reducing once normalized |
| **3** | **Overwork / "996" long working hours** | Weekly hours above ~50 | Promotion/income amenity; professional identity | Spouse and children bear relationship and caregiving cost; worker keeps income | Worker over-weights near-term wage/promotion vs long-run health | Career-capital lock-in; industry norms; fear of reversal signal |
| **4** | **Bride-price / wedding-consumption escalation** | Wedding expenditure level | In-group status; ceremonial satisfaction | Cost falls on 20-year household-balance-sheet and (in bride-price systems) on bride's natal family | Immediate ceremonial gratification vs long-run debt | Social-norm anchor; difficulty deviating once floor is set |
| **5** | **High-sugar/high-fat eating** | Calorie-dense food intake | Palatability; dopaminergic reward | Health cost partly shifted to public health system; self-as-future-person discounted | Immediate gustatory reward vs delayed metabolic cost | Habituation; reward-system adaptation; difficulty of reversal |
| **6** | **BNPL / consumer credit usage** | Credit-financed purchase frequency | Immediate consumption amenity | Lender bears default tail-risk; spouse/family bears debt-service burden | Immediate consumption vs deferred repayment | Minimum-payment anchor; credit-score signaling |
| **7** | **Short-video / social-media engagement** | Daily screen time | Dopaminergic variable-reward | Attention-economy platform internalizes advertising revenue; user bears attention cost | Immediate reward vs delayed productivity/sleep cost | Habit formation; platform algorithmic lock-in |
| **8** | **Premium housing / status-good purchase** | House-size / car-tier upgrade | Status amenity; social comparison | Debt service shared with household; future self bears opportunity cost | Immediate status vs long-run savings depletion | Neighborhood signaling; difficulty to downsize |

### Key claim: primitive structure, not domain specifics, is the construct

The four primitives are **domain-free abstractions**. A domain qualifies as a Sweet Trap instantiation if:

- (i) the primitive *interpretations* are substantively meaningful (not vacuous relabelings),
- (ii) D1–D6 can be operationalized in that domain's measurement system,
- (iii) at least one primitive is empirically estimable from within-person variation.

The 8 domains above all qualify. A domain that does *not* qualify — say, routine tax-filing behavior — would lack either θ (no first-order pleasure) or λ (no externalization) and is therefore excluded from the construct's scope.

### What the cross-domain matrix buys

Prior work on each individual domain exists (infrastructure: Song & Xiong 2026; parenting: Kim & Choi 2018; overwork: Fan et al. 2024; bride price: Ashraf et al. 2020; food: Dubois et al. 2020; credit: Stango & Zinman 2023; screens: Allcott et al. 2022; housing: Charles et al. 2009). **None** of these unifies the domains under a single primitive structure. The construct's contribution is precisely the unification: one parameter set, eight domains, same equilibrium signature.

## 4. Testable cross-domain predictions

The construct generates predictions that must hold across domains or the construct is falsified. These predictions are formulated to be domain-general and estimable from publicly available longitudinal survey data (CFPS, HRS, PSID, UKHLS, IFLS equivalents).

### Prediction 1 — Within-person endorsement (D1 × domain)

*Within-person fixed-effect regression of a well-being metric on domain engagement returns a positive coefficient in every candidate domain.*

- Formal: For each domain *d*, *β^{sat}_d ≡ ∂W_{i,1,d} / ∂I_{i,d} > 0* estimated with individual FE.
- Failure criterion: If in any single domain the coefficient is null or negative with adequate power, that domain exits the construct scope — but the construct survives if remaining domains hold.
- Inference rule: Positive in ≥ 5 of 8 domains with sign consistency > 80% in specification curves.

### Prediction 2 — Externalization heterogeneity (λ-driven)

*Within each domain, subgroups with structurally higher λ (greater externalization ability) exhibit strictly larger satisfaction-to-engagement slopes.*

- Formal: *∂β^{sat}_{i,d} / ∂λ_{i,d} > 0* in every domain.
- Operationalization of λ by domain:
  - Urban: age × migration expectation × cohort
  - Parenting: whether parent expects to support child in old age
  - Overwork: caregiving responsibility burden on spouse
  - Bride-price: whether groom's family vs bride's family bears cost
  - Food: public-insurance coverage ratio
  - Credit: household-head vs partner debt attribution
  - Screens: whether attention cost falls on employer/school
  - Housing: co-signer vs sole debtor
- Failure criterion: Null λ heterogeneity in ≥ half of domains = construct weakened; null in all domains = construct falsified.

### Prediction 3 — Cross-domain primitive stability

*The relative contribution of (θ, λ, β, ρ) to the wedge, estimated structurally, exhibits a non-random pattern across domains — specifically, λ and β contribute more in domains with longer temporal gaps between sweet and bitter.*

- Formal: Let *σ_d(θ), σ_d(λ), σ_d(β), σ_d(ρ)* be the share of the wedge attributed to each primitive in domain *d*. Across domains: *corr(σ_d(β), temporal_gap_d) > 0*, *corr(σ_d(λ), cohort_separation_d) > 0*.
- This prediction distinguishes Sweet Trap from any single-primitive competitor (if only hyperbolic discounting mattered, β would dominate in all domains; it does not).
- Failure criterion: If primitive decomposition is statistically indistinguishable from uniform random across domains, the construct is merely a multi-domain relabeling and should be dropped.

### Prediction 4 — Net-welfare quantification

*In each domain, the consumption-equivalent net welfare effect for a specifiable high-λ subgroup is negative.*

- Formal: For subgroup *G_d ⊂ i* with high λ, *ΔW_{CE,d,G} < 0* computed as:
  *ΔW_{CE} = ΔW_{short} + ΔW_{long} / (1+r)^t − costs*
  where short- and long-term components are estimated from the within-person FE coefficients and the welfare-cost literature.
- This prediction delivers the "consequence" leg of the Nature Human Behaviour narrative arc (Problem → Mechanism → Consequence).
- Failure criterion: If ΔW_{CE} > 0 in > half of domains, the construct loses policy relevance and narrative claim, even if D1–D6 hold statistically.

## 5. Scope conditions and boundary statements

The Sweet Trap is **not universal** across all decisions. It requires:

- **S1:** The choice dimension admits amenity (D3 / θ > 0). Purely instrumental choices without hedonic content do not produce traps.
- **S2:** The cost structure is intertemporally asymmetric (D5 / β < 1 matters) *and* has an externalization channel (D4 / λ > 0). Decisions where all costs are borne by the decision-maker in period 1 do not produce traps — this is where Sweet Trap differs from rational addiction.
- **S3:** The environment permits adaptive updating (D6 / ρ > 0). One-shot decisions with no repetition cannot exhibit lock-in.
- **S4:** The decision-maker has information sufficient to form period-2 beliefs; radical uncertainty over period-2 outcomes collapses the model to a single-period problem.

**What falls outside scope:**

- Purely cognitive biases with no equilibrium rationalization (e.g., anchoring in numerical estimation tasks)
- Decisions under full cost internalization (retirement savings by a single-adult household with no heirs)
- Decisions with positive period-2 welfare effects (genuine investment; the ST is defined precisely by D2 being negative)
- Decisions without a within-person well-being signature (bureaucratic compliance choices)

**What our Chinese political-economy paper (*infra-growth-mismatch*) establishes:** that the four primitives jointly activate in the Chinese urban-infrastructure domain, with the conditions in §5 of that paper (tournament promotion, Hukou mobility, LGFV debt, amenity-heavy infrastructure). That paper is the *first instantiation* of the Sweet Trap construct, not the construct itself.

## 6. Lessons from the urban paper: what this construct paper must avoid

The urban-infrastructure paper (`../infra-growth-mismatch/`) suffered three fatal presentation flaws documented in `phase5_synthesis_and_decision.md`. The construct paper must not reproduce them.

### Flaw 1: "Harmful" overclaiming

*Urban mistake:* Title and abstract used "Harmful Infrastructure Investment" but the growth-penalty evidence did not survive log(GDP) control in the SI.

*Construct-paper rule:* Never characterize the Sweet option as "harmful" without the long-run welfare leg being statistically robust. The construct is defined by *timing asymmetry* and *intergenerational transfer*, not by a unilateral "harm" verdict. Preferred language: "welfare-redistributive", "intergenerationally-regressive", "asymmetric-timing".

### Flaw 2: Headline coefficient cherry-picking

*Urban mistake:* Abstract reported β = +0.486 (Model 3, one of four PDE specifications) without disclosing that three other specifications returned p > 0.05 and that the specification-curve median was +0.192.

*Construct-paper rule:* Every cross-domain claim in the main text must report the specification-curve median effect, not the favored point estimate. For each of 5+ focal domains we pre-specify the identification, pre-register the specification curve, and report median ± IQR. Domain-specific best models are confined to SI.

### Flaw 3: Post-hoc rescue of a pre-registered null

*Urban mistake:* Study 5's pre-registered hukou-mobility λ test returned a sign-inverted null, which was then reframed with a post-hoc three-way interaction presented as supporting evidence.

*Construct-paper rule:* Each domain's pre-registration is submitted to OSF *before* PDE begins. Prediction 2 (λ-heterogeneity) will be tested domain-by-domain with pre-specified λ proxies. If a λ-proxy returns a null in a specific domain, the null is reported as the primary result; no post-hoc subsampling is permitted in the main text.

### Operational constraint for the construct paper

Because we have eight candidate domains, the probability that at least one returns a favored-looking coefficient by chance is high. We therefore commit in advance to:

- **Pre-registering** the identification strategy and headline specification for each of 5+ focal domains (OSF, before analysis)
- **Reporting** full specification-curve analysis (≥ 500 variants per domain)
- **Using** the cross-domain *count* of confirmations at the pre-specified threshold (not individual-domain point estimates) as the primary evidence statistic
- **Treating** any single-domain confirmation as insufficient; cross-domain consistency is the construct's evidentiary standard

This converts the multi-domain design from a garden-of-forking-paths liability into an asset: the 8-domain structure provides a *within-study multiple-testing correction*, increasing rather than decreasing the evidentiary bar for the construct.

## 7. Why this is the level the construct paper should target

The construct as defined above is:

- **Sufficiently abstract** to accommodate 8 structurally different domains (urban policy, parenting, labor supply, marriage, food, credit, media, housing) without forcing a single-domain framing.
- **Sufficiently specific** to exclude rational addiction (via λ > 0), status-quo bias (via θ + β), hyperbolic discounting (via λ + ρ), and adaptive preferences (via active endorsement).
- **Sufficiently falsifiable** to generate five distinct failure conditions (F1–F5) and four cross-domain predictions (P1–P4), each estimable on publicly available longitudinal survey data.
- **Sufficiently policy-relevant** to connect to an active literature (behavioral public economics: Bernheim & Taubinsky 2018; Chetty 2015; Allcott, Lockwood & Taubinsky 2019) and to current policy windows (debt sustainability, mental-health crisis in young workers, child-development concerns).

The construct's signature novelty — and the claim to Nature Human Behaviour — is that it unifies under one primitive structure a set of phenomena currently analyzed in ~8 separate literatures, each with its own vocabulary, each missing the externalization pillar that makes the endorsement *rational* rather than mistaken.

## 8. Notation and terminology (for downstream documents)

| Symbol | Meaning | Domain of variation |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| *I* | Engagement / investment / consumption intensity | Domain-specific scalar |
| *W_1*, *W_2* | Period-1 well-being, period-2 welfare | Within-person panel |
| *θ* | Amenity weight (primitive 1) | Domain constant or slowly varying |
| *λ_i* | Externalization share (primitive 2) | Individual and time-varying |
| *β_i* | Quasi-hyperbolic present-bias (primitive 3) | Individual |
| *ρ* | Status-quo lock-in intensity (primitive 4) | Domain constant |
| *I** | Social-planner first-best | Domain constant |
| *I*_i* | Agent-optimal | Individual-specific |
| *β^{sat}_{i,d}* | Individual-domain satisfaction slope | Estimated within-person |
| *ΔW_{CE,d,G}* | Consumption-equivalent net welfare, domain d, subgroup G | Quantified per Prediction 4 |

"Sweet" = period-1 amenity / momentary well-being gain.
"Bitter" = period-2 welfare loss (health, wealth, mental health, equivalent).
"Trap" = endurance of I* > I** in equilibrium, resistant to information provision, reinforced by ρ > 0.
