## Why Sweet Trap Cannot Be Reduced to Any Single Adjacent Construct

### Cannot be reduced to Mismatch Theory
Mismatch theory explains *why* modern bodies experience disease (ancestral calibration × novel environment). It does not explain:
- Why organisms *choose and endorse* the mismatched stimulus (the endorsement condition)
- Why the harmful choice persists after exposure to consequences (absent corrective feedback)
- Why group-level equilibria are stable (the social/institutional lock-in)

A mismatch disease (e.g., diabetes) happens *to* an organism passively. A Sweet Trap is actively endorsed and self-reinforced. This is the core distinction.

### Cannot be reduced to Evolutionary Trap
Evolutionary trap describes habitat or resource selection error in novel environments. It does not include:
- The active *endorsement* of the choice (the organism preferring the trap actively, with subjective satisfaction)
- The *self-reinforcing equilibrium* (the preference strengthening through experience and cultural transmission)
- Application to *non-habitat domains* (economic, social, institutional traps)
- A *formal economic model* enabling welfare quantification

Robertson et al. 2013 explicitly call for formal models and do not provide one. Sweet Trap fills this exact gap.

### Cannot be reduced to Behavioral Economics (Internality)
Behavioral economics frames welfare-reducing choices as *biases* — deviations from rational self-interest that could be corrected if agents had better information, commitment devices, or nudges. Sweet Trap:
- Does NOT require any bias. A fully rational agent (in the economic sense) can be in a Sweet Trap if their *evolutionarily calibrated reward function* has decoupled from fitness.
- Explains *why* nudges systematically fail in specific domains (high reward signal domains): the reward signal overwhelms nudge-induced preference changes.
- Provides an *evolutionary account* of why certain biases (present bias, loss aversion) exist and why they are cross-species — behavioral economics treats these as anomalies; Sweet Trap treats them as evolutionary adaptations in novel contexts.

### Cannot be reduced to Addiction
Addiction is Sweet Trap at its extreme: near-total reward-fitness decoupling, loss of voluntary control, clinical pathology. But:
- Sweet Trap includes non-pathological, socially endorsed behaviors (996 culture, 鸡娃, brideprice) where the individual is fully functional and perceives their choice as rational and status-affirming.
- Addiction literature focuses on the *individual neural substrate*; Sweet Trap focuses on *population-level equilibria* maintained by cultural transmission and institutional incentives.
- Sweet Trap explains why the "addiction-like" pattern occurs in behaviors not traditionally labeled addictions: the mechanism is identical (reward signal decoupled from fitness, self-reinforcing) but the context is social/economic rather than chemical.

### Cannot be reduced to Fisher Runaway
Runaway selection is a genetic/evolutionary mechanism operating over generations. Sweet Trap:
- Operates within-generation (individual decision level)
- Does not require genetic changes — cultural/institutional mechanisms produce the same pattern much faster
- Applies to all reward-domain choices, not just mate preferences
- Is formally modeled with economic utility functions, not just population genetics

Runaway selection is the evolutionary biology *prototype* of Sweet Trap; Sweet Trap is the generalization to all timescales, all domains, all species with reward systems.

## How to Describe the Positioning in One Paragraph (Nature Introduction Draft)

"Previous frameworks have described components of this phenomenon in isolation: evolutionary and ecological traps document habitat-selection errors in non-human species when novel environments mismatch ancestral cues [Schlaepfer 2002; Robertson 2013]; mismatch theory describes the physiological consequences when ancestral adaptations encounter modern conditions [Nesse 1994]; behavioral economics characterizes the internalities generated by present-biased preferences [Laibson 1997; Bernheim 2018]; and the neuroscience of reward identifies the dissociation between dopaminergic wanting and hedonic liking [Berridge 1998]. However, no framework unifies these observations under a single formal mechanism operating across species and domains, explains why these patterns are self-reinforcing at individual and population levels, and specifies the conditions under which reward signals decouple from fitness consequences. Here we propose the Sweet Trap — ancestrally adaptive reward signal decoupled from current-environment fitness consequences — as this unifying principle, and demonstrate its operation from moth phototaxis to human overwork."

## Reviewer Risk Map

**Reviewer 1 attack: "This is just mismatch theory."**
Our defense: Mismatch theory describes a passive physiological response; Sweet Trap requires active endorsement and predicts self-reinforcing equilibria. We model the difference formally.

**Reviewer 2 attack: "This is just the ecological/evolutionary trap literature repackaged."**
Our defense: Evolutionary traps lack (a) the endorsement condition, (b) the formal economic model, (c) multi-domain generalization, (d) quantified human welfare consequences. We explicitly build on that literature and identify its limitations in our Introduction.

**Reviewer 3 attack: "The behavioral economics framing makes this a behavioral economics paper, not a Nature paper."**
Our defense: Behavioral economics is our method in the human domain; the theory is evolutionary biology. The same pattern (reward-fitness decoupling) appears in moths, frogs, fruit flies, rats, and humans — this is not a behavioral economics paper, it is an evolutionary biology paper with human economic validation.

**Reviewer 4 attack: "You claim cross-species universality but only do human empirics."**
Our defense: (a) We document the mechanism across multiple non-human species in the Results (ecological trap review); (b) the human panel data tests the formal predictions at unprecedented scale; (c) the neural conservation evidence (Science 2025, Nature 2023) shows the mechanism operates at the biological level across species.
