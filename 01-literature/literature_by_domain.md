## Construct Reference: Sweet Trap Four Primitives

From `infra-growth-mismatch/00-design/formal_model.md` v4, the Sweet Trap requires:
- **θ** (amenity preference): immediate consumption utility of the good/activity
- **λ** (externalization): fraction of long-run cost shifted to others / future self
- **β** (present bias): quasi-hyperbolic discounting over-weighting period-1 gain
- **ρ** (status-quo lock-in): preference updating toward the chosen action

Sweet Trap evidence = empirical documentation that individuals **rationally endorse** choices that **short-term raise** and **long-run reduce** welfare, AND show one or more of (θ, λ, β, ρ) as the mechanism.

### 3 Most Relevant Papers

2. **Rogoff, K., & Yang, Y. (2024).** Peak China housing. NBER Working Paper.

3. **Chen, Z., He, Z., & Liu, C. (2020).** The financing of local government in China. *Journal of Financial Economics*, 137(1), 42–71.
   - LGFV debt mechanics: off-budget, deferred burden, shadow banking amplification.
   - Our λ-mechanism source: deferred financing is precisely why externalization is structurally built in.

### Gap Analysis for Study 1
**Already closed.** The within-person behavioral demand-side identification is our contribution. No gap remains — this is the anchor Study 1 for the multi-domain paper.

## Domain 2: 鸡娃教育 (Intensive Parenting / Shadow Education)

### Sweet Trap Pattern Reported? PARTIAL — scattered evidence, no unified Sweet Trap framing

**Existing evidence:**
- "Academic arms race" documented in China (双减 policy context); shadow education expenditure growing 15-20% annually pre-2021.
- Child mental health consequences documented (anxiety, depression) — but generally in child psychology journals with weak causal identification.
- Parental endorsement despite observed child distress — documented qualitatively (Fong 2004, "Only Hope"; ethnographic) but NOT formally quantified as a welfare paradox.
- No within-person panel study showing parents simultaneously report pride/satisfaction AND children show declining wellbeing.

### 3 Most Relevant Papers

1. **Heckman, J. J., & Mosso, S. (2014).** The economics of human development and social mobility. *Annual Review of Economics*, 6, 689–733.
   - Early childhood investment produces high returns; foundational for "education investment = good" framing.
   - Our difference: this is the legitimate-investment benchmark. Sweet Trap is about over-investment beyond first-best — when returns are negative at the margin but parents keep endorsing.

2. **Zhang, W. (2020).** The effects of shadow education on academic achievement in East Asia: A critical review. *Educational Research Review*, 30, 100323.
   - Documents that shadow tutoring in China/Korea/Japan raises test scores modestly but at large cost; parental WTP consistently exceeds expected returns.
   - Contains Sweet Trap ingredients (θ high, λ via child bearing cost, β via status competition) but does not apply the construct.
   - Our increment: unify into Sweet Trap + within-person CFPS identification.

3. **Briggs, D. C. (2009).** Preparation for college admission exams. *DICE Research Report*. (Updated: Pop-Eleches, C., & Urquiola, M. (2013), AER) — Urquiola, M., & Pop-Eleches, C. (2013). Going to a better school. *AER*, 103(4), 1289–1324.
   - Regression discontinuity: students just above/below selective school cutoff. Those who just make a selective school do not have better outcomes than those who just miss it (labor market returns ≈ 0 at margin).
   - Critical: shows the marginal investment in academic competition yields near-zero return while generating large parental expenditure.
   - Our increment: parental satisfaction (θ) remains high even when returns approach zero — that is the Sweet Trap.

### Additional Relevant (not in corpus-index)
- **Kuan, P.-Y. (2011).** Effects of cram schooling on mathematics performance: Evidence from junior high students in Taiwan. *Economics of Education Review*, 30(5), 1079–1092. — Negative returns to excessive tutoring on wellbeing, mixed on scores.
- **Xie, Y., & Zhou, X. (2014).** Income inequality in today's China. *PNAS*, 111(19), 6928–6933. — Status anxiety in China drives over-investment in children as positional good.

### Gap Analysis
Three gaps:
1. **Measurement**: No study formally measures parental endorsement (satisfaction, pride) simultaneously with child outcome data within the same panel. CFPS has both.
2. **Identification**: Existing literature uses cross-sectional or selection-biased designs. CFPS within-person FE (parent ID over waves) can purge selection.
3. **Mechanism**: β and θ implicated but never formally parameterized. λ = intergenerational cost transfer (financial strain → child) is novel operationalization.

### Preliminary Claim
Domain 2 has scattered evidence that is consistent with Sweet Trap but has never applied the construct or used within-person identification to show the endorsement-harm paradox. Medium evidence maturity. Strong novelty potential.

## Domain 3: 996 过劳工作 (Long Working Hours / Overwork)

### Sweet Trap Pattern Reported? PARTIAL — health/family consequences documented; rational endorsement not formally modeled

**Existing evidence:**
- Long working hours → cardiovascular disease, depression documented in large epidemiological studies (Kivimäki et al. 2015, Lancet: N=600,000; dose-response for CVD).
- Income premium for overwork documented (Goldin, 2014, AER P&P; "Greedy Jobs") — workers choose overwork partly for pay and career advancement.
- "Workaholism" literature exists in organizational psychology but lacks welfare economics framing.
- 996 specific (China 9am-9pm, 6 days/week): documented qualitatively, no large-panel welfare analysis.
- The rational endorsement piece is implied (workers voluntarily choose) but nobody has shown that workers with higher overwork simultaneously show higher short-run career satisfaction AND lower long-run family/health wellbeing WITHIN PERSON.

### 3 Most Relevant Papers

1. **Goldin, C. (2014).** A grand gender convergence: Its last chapter. *American Economic Review*, 104(4), 1091–1119.
   - "Greedy jobs" premium: nonlinear pay-to-hours schedule makes overwork individually rational even when collectively suboptimal.
   - Contains ρ (lock-in: once you take greedy job, exiting is costly) and β (present-bias toward immediate career rewards).
   - Our difference: Goldin focuses on gender wage gap, not welfare reduction. We focus on the endorsement-harm paradox within person.

2. **Kivimäki, M., et al. (2015).** Long working hours and risk of coronary heart disease and stroke: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Lancet*, 386(10005), 1739–1746.
   - Meta-analysis N≈600,000; long hours (≥55/week) raises CVD risk 13% and stroke risk 33%. Dose-response established.
   - This is the λ quantification: health cost externalizing from current-work-self to future-sick-self.
   - Our difference: they document costs, not the paradox of endorsement. Workers are not in this meta-analysis asked whether they would do it again.

3. **Pencavel, J. (2015).** The productivity of working hours. *Economic Journal*, 125(589), 2052–2076.
   - With WWI munitions data: productivity per hour declines sharply beyond ~50 hours/week; >55 hours essentially zero marginal productivity.
   - Shows over-work is objectively less productive — yet workers (and employers) rationally sustain it due to signaling/coordination.
   - Sweet Trap ingredient: ρ status-quo (once norms are set at 996, deviation is career-costly).

### Additional Relevant
- **Cha, Y., & Weeden, K. A. (2014).** Overwork and the slow convergence in the gender gap in wages. *American Sociological Review*, 79(3), 457–484.
- **Schor, J. B. (1991).** *The Overworked American.* Basic Books. — Classic statement of the collective action problem in overwork.

### Gap Analysis
Three gaps:
1. **Measurement**: No study measures within-person career satisfaction (θ endorsement) simultaneously with health/family outcomes in a longitudinal Chinese panel.
2. **Identification**: Causal identification of overwork → wellbeing requires within-person variation; CFPS work module provides hours + satisfaction + health over waves.
3. **Mechanism**: β + λ together not formally tested. The λ channel (cost to family members = spillover) is measurable in CFPS family module.

### Preliminary Claim
Domain 3 has the richest health-cost evidence of any domain but the weakest formal welfare economics framing. Sweet Trap pattern is implied but the paradox of rational endorsement is never directly tested. High novelty potential for NHB if we show that workers who work longest hours have highest career pride AND worst family outcomes simultaneously.

## Domain 4: 彩礼/婚恋消费 (Bride Price / Wedding Expenditure)

### Sweet Trap Pattern Reported? WEAK — welfare consequences of bride price documented; endorsement paradox absent

**Existing evidence:**
- Bride price / dowry in sub-Saharan Africa: large economics literature (Anderson 2007, JEL review; Ashraf et al. 2020 on female empowerment). Generally shows bride price correlates with worse female outcomes (domestic violence, lower autonomy).
- China-specific: bride price (彩礼) rising dramatically since 2000s — documented in sociology (Lavely 2001; Hackney 2014). Estimates: median bride price in rural China ~3-5x annual household income by 2018.
- Wedding expenditure and post-wedding debt: documented in consumer finance literature, no large-panel causal study.
- **Critically missing**: No study shows that families who paid high bride price report high satisfaction with the marriage/ritual (θ endorsement) while also showing long-run household financial distress (λ cost externalization to future household consumption).

### 3 Most Relevant Papers

1. **Anderson, S. (2007).** The economics of dowry and brideprice. *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 21(4), 151–174.
   - Definitive JEP review of market-clearing theory vs. matching theory of marriage payments.
   - Documents welfare consequences (women's autonomy, domestic violence risk) but doesn't address the paradox of endorsement — why families voluntarily pay.
   - Our increment: formal Sweet Trap parameterization: ρ (social norms = status-quo bias), θ (ceremonial pride), λ (burden to next generation through household debt).

2. **Ashraf, N., Bau, N., Low, C., & McGinn, K. (2020).** Negotiating a better future: How interpersonal skills facilitate intergenerational investment. *QJE*, 135(2), 1095–1151.
   - Shows bride price in Zambia → higher female empowerment in some contexts via negotiation channel.
   - Our difference: their mechanism is about bargaining power, not Sweet Trap. China context (rural, high norms pressure) is the ρ-dominant case — negotiation is weak.

3. **Wei, S.-J., & Zhang, X. (2011).** The competitive saving motive: Evidence from rising sex ratios and savings rates in China. *Journal of Political Economy*, 119(3), 511–564.
   - Sex-ratio imbalance → precautionary savings to afford marriage competition; shows marriage competition distorts household savings over a generation.
   - Closest to Sweet Trap: the arms-race dynamic is explicit. But they focus on savings rates, not the satisfaction-harm paradox within person.

### Gap Analysis
Three gaps:
1. **Measurement**: CFPS has wedding expenditure module (1999-2019 waves), marital satisfaction, and household financial outcomes. No study has linked all three within-person.
2. **Identification**: Bride price norms vary by region (high in rural Shandong/Shaanxi, low in urban coastal). Geographic variation + temporal variation provide exogenous shifters.
3. **Mechanism**: ρ (norm compliance) as Sweet Trap mechanism is undertheorized in China context. Connecting social norm enforcement to the formal model is novel.

### Preliminary Claim
Domain 4 has good background evidence (JEP review, QJE, JPE) but NO within-person Chinese panel study linking bride price endorsement to long-run household welfare. Gap is measurement + identification. However, CFPS coverage of this domain may be limited — needs variable audit before committing.

## Domain 5: 高糖高脂饮食 (Ultra-Processed / High-Sugar Diet)

### Sweet Trap Pattern Reported? YES (most mature domain outside urban) — but without Sweet Trap framing

**Existing evidence:**
- Sugar/fat palatability → immediate reward is among the best-understood neuroscience findings. Gut-brain axis mediates sugar preference (Nature 2020, 10.1038/s41586-020-2199-7; N=cites 299) — dopamine-driven anticipatory reward.
- Hyperbolic discounting and food choice: canonical behavioral economics demonstration domain. Fudenberg & Levine (2006, AER P&P) use food as the paradigmatic β-δ application.
- Long-run health consequences: massive epidemiological literature. UPF (ultra-processed food) → all-cause mortality, CVD, diabetes — documented in large cohort studies (Fiolet et al. 2018, BMJ N=105K; Lane et al. 2022 EClinicalMedicine).
- "Rational addiction" applied to food: Cawley & Ruhm (2011, NBER) show food consumption consistent with intertemporal rational choice but yielding negative long-run health outcomes.
- **What exists**: individual-level present bias in food choice + population-level health cost. **What does not exist**: a within-person longitudinal study showing the same individuals simultaneously report high immediate enjoyment (θ endorsement measure) AND deteriorating health outcomes — the Sweet Trap paradox within person.

### 3 Most Relevant Papers

1. **Cawley, J., & Ruhm, C. J. (2011).** The economics of risky health behaviors. *Handbook of Health Economics* (Vol. 2, pp. 95–199). Elsevier.
   - Comprehensive framework: rational addiction (Becker-Murphy), self-control, present-bias all applied to food. Shows market equilibrium with overconsumption is individually rational.
   - Closest to Sweet Trap framing — but a theoretical handbook chapter, no large-panel empirical identification.
   - Our increment: CFPS provides within-person panel with dietary behavior, BMI/diabetes outcomes, and satisfaction — direct empirical test.

2. **Monteiro, C. A., et al. (2019).** Ultra-processed foods: What they are and how to identify them. *Public Health Nutrition*, 22(5), 936–941. (NOVA classification)
   - Operational definition of UPF enabling population-level measurement.
   - Methodological anchor — we use NOVA-compatible measures in CFPS dietary module.

3. **Hall, K. D., et al. (2019).** Ultra-processed diets cause excess calorie intake and weight gain: An inpatient randomized controlled trial. *Cell Metabolism*, 30(1), 67–77.
   - N=20 RCT; ad libitum UPF diet → +508 kcal/day, +0.9kg in 2 weeks. Gold-standard causal evidence that UPF increases caloric intake and promotes weight gain.
   - Our complement: this establishes the causal direction (UPF → weight gain); we provide the within-person satisfaction-endorsement piece at population scale that the RCT cannot.

### Gap Analysis
Three gaps:
1. **Measurement**: No large-N panel study measures immediate food enjoyment/endorsement AND long-run health trajectory within person in low/middle income country context (where transition is accelerating).
2. **Identification**: China's dietary transition (rapid UPF adoption post-2000, CFPS tracks this) creates temporal variation. Within-person FE purges stable preference.
3. **Mechanism**: β is the dominant mechanism but λ (cost externalization to health system / future self / family) is not formally parameterized in the food domain.

### Preliminary Claim
Domain 5 is the most conceptually mature domain for Sweet Trap outside urban. The individual mechanisms are well-established; the integrated within-person endorsement-harm paradox at population scale in a Chinese panel is the gap. CFPS dietary module needs validation. High probability of finding the pattern.

## Domain 6: BNPL / 信用消费 (Buy Now Pay Later / Impulsive Credit Use)

### Sweet Trap Pattern Reported? PARTIAL — emerging literature, weak causal identification

**Existing evidence:**
- BNPL industry exploded globally 2018-2023 (Afterpay, Klarna, etc.); consumer research documents: (a) increased spending under BNPL vs. cash, (b) higher default/financial distress rates.
- Behavioral: BNPL works by making present costs invisible (β mechanism: future repayment installments look small). Clegg et al. (2023, JPSP) lab experiments show BNPL increases willingness to pay 20-30%.
- Consumer finance literature: credit card debt and wellbeing. Gathergood (2012, EJ) documents that over-indebtedness reduces life satisfaction; Brown et al. (2013, JME) shows credit access increases consumption but reduces savings.
- **Missing**: No longitudinal within-person study (CFPS-equivalent) linking initial credit-enabled purchase satisfaction to subsequent household financial distress AND back to endorsement of continued credit use. The "rational endorsement" side is weak.

### 3 Most Relevant Papers

1. **Gathergood, J. (2012).** Self-control, financial literacy and consumer over-indebtedness. *Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization*, 80(1), 25–33.
   - Links low financial literacy + self-control problems to over-indebtedness; shows over-indebted households report lower life satisfaction.
   - Contains β mechanism + welfare loss. Missing: endorsement paradox (do they want to keep using credit even after knowing the harm?).
   - Our increment: within-person identification + formal Sweet Trap parameterization.

2. **Agarwal, S., Driscoll, J. C., Gabaix, X., & Laibson, D. (2009).** The age of reason: Financial decisions over the life cycle. *Brookings Papers on Economic Activity*, 2009(2), 51–117.
   - Shows financial mistakes (including credit card misuse) peak at age 42 (inverted U) — implies present bias combined with experience learning.
   - β mechanism empirically demonstrated at scale. Our Sweet Trap extension: show that credit-use satisfaction persists even after financial distress onset.

3. **Brown, S., Taylor, K., & Price, S. W. (2005).** Debt and distress: Evaluating the psychological cost of credit. *Journal of Economic Psychology*, 26(5), 642–663.
   - N=8,468 UK; unsecured debt → psychological distress. Dose-response. Foundational welfare cost evidence.
   - Our difference: British cross-section vs. within-person Chinese panel longitudinal. Chinese BNPL/credit card adoption post-2015 is a natural temporal experiment.

### Gap Analysis
Three gaps:
1. **Measurement**: CFPS has household debt module (consumer credit, credit card), consumption satisfaction, and mental health outcomes. No study links all three in China.
2. **Identification**: Rapid credit card adoption post-2015 + BNPL expansion post-2018 in China provides temporal variation. Within-person FE identifies the effect.
3. **Mechanism**: β is dominant in theory; λ (externalizing to family) is measurable via CFPS family financial transfers module.

### Preliminary Claim
Domain 6 is data-limited in CFPS (the consumption satisfaction measure may be proxied rather than direct). The BNPL-specific mechanism is very recent. Sweet Trap framing is novel. But CFPS waves pre-2022 may undercount modern BNPL behavior. Lower CFPS data quality expected — flag for Stage 1 data audit.

## Domain 7: 短视频/社媒 (Short Video / Social Media)

### Sweet Trap Pattern Reported? YES (most debated domain) — the endorsement-harm paradox is the CORE debate

**Existing evidence:**
This is the domain where the Sweet Trap pattern is most explicitly theorized (though not named as such) and most actively contested:
- **Orben & Przybylski (2019, NHB)**: Specification curve analysis of screen time and adolescent wellbeing; effect sizes tiny (≈ 0.05 SD); causal story weak. This is the main "no harm" paper.
- **Haidt & Allen (2020, Nature)**: Commentary rebutting Orben-Przybylski; argues for harm. The debate is live.
- **Braghieri, L., Levy, R., & Palomino, A. (2022, AER)**: Facebook rollout as natural experiment; college mental health data; Facebook access reduced mental health by 0.3 SD. Best causal identification in domain.
- **Allcott et al. (2020, QJE)**: Facebook deactivation RCT (N=2,844); deactivation improved wellbeing but users chose to re-activate post-study. **This is the closest existing Sweet Trap evidence**: users rationally chose to return to a platform that demonstrably reduced their wellbeing.
- **Dopamine / reward loop**: Theoretical basis well-established (Ding et al. 2019, Nature Comms; Ward et al. 2017, JEP).

### 3 Most Relevant Papers

1. **Allcott, H., Braghieri, L., Bursztyn, L., & Gentzkow, M. (2020).** The welfare effects of social media. *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 135(1), 211–254.
   - Facebook deactivation RCT: deactivation → +15 min/day offline social, −22 min/day news, improved subjective wellbeing. BUT re-activation rate high post-study (users know cost, still return).
   - **This is the closest existing Sweet Trap paper**: rational re-endorsement after demonstrated harm. They call this "subjective misallocation" — we call it Sweet Trap.
   - Our difference: they focus on Facebook only, US context, one-time RCT. We want within-person longitudinal panel across Chinese short video (Douyin) context, multi-wave.

2. **Braghieri, L., Levy, R. E., & Palomino, A. (2022).** Social media and mental health. *American Economic Review*, 112(11), 3660–3693.
   - Staggered Facebook rollout across US colleges 2004-2006 as natural experiment; mental health index declined 0.3 SD; depression +7%; anxiety +20%.
   - Best causal identification in the literature. λ mechanism: costs fall on future self (mental health deteriorates over years of use).
   - Our difference: Chinese Douyin/short video context (algorithmic recommendation > organic social), CFPS panel tracking, within-person β identification.

3. **Orben, A., & Przybylski, A. K. (2019).** The association between adolescent well-being and digital technology use. *Nature Human Behaviour*, 3(2), 173–182.
   - 355,358 participants; specification curve across 40,966 model variants; 89.4% of specifications negative but effect sizes trivially small (0.4-4% variance explained vs. wearing glasses 0.2%).
   - This is the adversarial benchmark we must differentiate from. NHB editors will ask why our approach improves on this.
   - Our difference: (1) within-person FE vs. cross-sectional; (2) Chinese panel (Orben uses Western YRBS/MCS); (3) we measure endorsement-endorsement trajectory, not static association.

### Gap Analysis
Three gaps:
1. **Measurement**: No within-person Chinese panel study tracks short-video use, immediate enjoyment endorsement, AND longitudinal wellbeing deterioration simultaneously.
2. **Identification**: Allcott et al. (2020) RCT is gold standard but one-time cross-sectional; CFPS provides multi-wave within-person FE with longer horizon.
3. **Mechanism**: The re-endorsement after harm (Allcott's key finding) is Sweet Trap in disguise — but they don't operationalize it as β + ρ explicitly. We formalize it.

### Preliminary Claim
Domain 7 is the most actively contested domain and has the **closest existing Sweet Trap paper** (Allcott et al. 2020). Our NHB value-add is: (1) Chinese context (Douyin vs. Facebook), (2) within-person longitudinal identification (vs. RCT snapshot), (3) explicit Sweet Trap parameterization. The Orben-Przybylski small-effect-size finding must be confronted directly — our within-person design is specifically superior.

**Warning**: Reviewer will cite Allcott et al. (2020) and say "you're just replicating this." Differentiation must emphasize: longitudinal multi-wave vs. one-time RCT; endorsement-over-time dynamic vs. static welfare; Chinese Douyin algorithmic context vs. organic social network.

## Domain 8: 高档住房/车消费 (Conspicuous Housing / Vehicle Consumption)

### Sweet Trap Pattern Reported? PARTIAL — individual pieces exist but no unified panel welfare analysis

**Existing evidence:**
- **Positional goods / status competition**: Hirsch (1976) "Social Limits to Growth" — seminal statement that positional goods generate welfare-destroying arms races. Frank (1985, 2007 "Falling Behind") operationalizes.
- **Housing and wellbeing**: Dietz & Haurin (2003, JUE) — homeownership associated with wellbeing; but also Oswald & Wu (2011, Science) — homeownership reduces labor mobility. Mixed.
- **Mortgage stress**: Nettleton & Burrows (1998, Sociology); Wood et al. (2015, HUS) — high mortgage-to-income → anxiety, depression. UK/Australian evidence.
- **Chinese housing**: Fang et al. (2016, AER) document the Chinese housing boom; house price expectations. But no welfare paradox study.
- **Vehicle**: Adams (2023, AER) status signaling in car consumption. Brülhart & Parchet (2014, AEJ:AE) status consumption in cross-sectional wellbeing.
- **Missing**: No within-person longitudinal study showing: high-status purchase → immediate satisfaction spike → medium-run debt stress → continued endorsement of status purchase.

### 3 Most Relevant Papers

1. **Frank, R. H. (2007).** *Falling Behind: How Rising Inequality Harms the Middle Class.* UC Press.
   - Theoretical statement of positional-goods arms race: everyone buys bigger houses because neighbors do; welfare loss from collectively suboptimal equilibrium.
   - ρ mechanism (status-quo: if you don't keep up, social costs) + θ mechanism (direct utility from status).
   - Our difference: Frank is theoretical/discursive; we want within-person empirical identification.

2. **Luttmer, E. F. P. (2005).** Neighbors as negatives: Relative income and well-being. *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 120(3), 963–1002.
   - N=30K NSFH; neighbors' income negatively affects own wellbeing (reference dependence). Shows positional welfare competition is empirically real.
   - θ mechanism: consumption utility is partly relative, amplifying over-investment in status goods.
   - Our difference: relative income → wellbeing (cross-sectional comparison domain); we want housing/vehicle × within-person × debt trajectory.

3. **Fang, H., Gu, Q., Xiong, W., & Zhou, L.-A. (2016).** Demystifying the Chinese housing boom. *NBER Macro Annual*, 31, 105–166.
   - Documents expectations-driven Chinese housing boom; investment motives dominant over consumption motives.
   - λ mechanism: investment housing converts welfare cost from own-consumption to future price risk externalized to latecomers.
   - Our difference: they focus on investment channel; we focus on consumption channel (owner-occupier satisfaction vs. debt stress).

### Gap Analysis
Three gaps:
1. **Measurement**: CFPS has housing value, mortgage status, car ownership, consumption satisfaction, and financial anxiety over waves. No study links all.
2. **Identification**: Chinese housing boom created exogenous price variation that pushed households into different LTV (loan-to-value) positions — natural variation in λ.
3. **Mechanism**: ρ (status-quo lock-in once you buy the prestige apartment, downgrading is welfare-costly) + θ (direct status utility) are both measurable via CFPS.

### Preliminary Claim
Domain 8 has rich theoretical background but weak within-person empirical identification. CFPS housing module is the asset data available in most waves. The positional arms-race story (Frank, Luttmer) is the Sweet Trap at the collective level — we individualize it with within-person panel evidence. Moderate novelty, strong data fit.

## Behavioral Economics Foundations: Required Citations

These four papers are the **mandatory theoretical anchors** for the Sweet Trap framework and must appear in the Introduction regardless of domain selection:

### BEC-1: Quasi-hyperbolic discounting (β mechanism)
**Laibson, D. (1997).** Golden eggs and hyperbolic discounting. *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 112(2), 443–477.
- β-δ preference formalization; present-bias; canonical reference.

**O'Donoghue, T., & Rabin, M. (1999).** Doing it now or later. *American Economic Review*, 89(1), 103–124.
- Sophisticated vs. naive present-biased agents; procrastination; "cue-triggered" overconsumption.

### BEC-2: Rational addiction (intertemporal consistent overconsumption)
**Becker, G. S., & Murphy, K. M. (1988).** A theory of rational addiction. *Journal of Political Economy*, 96(4), 675–700.
- Adjacent construct: rational addiction = intertemporal optimization with habit formation. Sweet Trap differs in that the full-information rational agent still ends up over-consuming; it is not about irrationality.
- Must cite and differentiate: in Sweet Trap, the rational agent has correct beliefs about future harm but endorses due to θ + λ + β + ρ interacting.

### BEC-3: Behavioral welfare economics / internalities
**Bernheim, B. D., & Taubinsky, D. (2018).** Behavioral public economics. *Handbook of Behavioral Economics*, 1, 381–516.
- Defines "internalities" = self-imposed welfare costs due to behavioral failures. Our Sweet Trap is a specific type of internality.
- Chetty, R. (2015). Behavioral economics and public policy: A pragmatic perspective. *AER*, 105(5), 1–33.

### BEC-4: Status-quo bias / endowment effect (ρ mechanism)
**Samuelson, W., & Zeckhauser, R. (1988).** Status quo bias in decision making. *Journal of Risk and Uncertainty*, 1, 7–59.
**Kahneman, D., Knetsch, J. L., & Thaler, R. H. (1991).** Anomalies: The endowment effect, loss aversion, and status quo bias. *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 5(1), 193–206.

### BEC-5: Hedonic adaptation (baseline for "no Sweet Trap" null)
**Frederick, S., & Loewenstein, G. (1999).** Hedonic adaptation. In D. Kahneman, E. Diener, & N. Schwarz (Eds.), *Well-Being: The Foundations of Hedonic Psychology* (pp. 302–329).
- If hedonic adaptation fully operates, there should be no sustained satisfaction from Sweet Trap good → adaptation would eliminate the θ term. Sweet Trap requires adaptation to be partial/slow.
