## Genre Definition: What Makes a Nature/Science Cross-Species Paper

From the corpus analysis, the "cross-species universal mechanism" genre in Nature/Science has three variants:

**Type A: Neural conservation** — Same neural circuit, same function, conserved across phylogenetic distance (rodent-primate-human comparisons). Home journal: Nature / Nature Neuroscience / Science.

**Type B: Behavioral convergence** — Independent evolution of same behavioral strategy across ecologically distant species. Home journal: Science / Nature Ecology & Evolution.

**Type C: Evolutionary mechanism paper** — Theory paper showing that a single evolutionary mechanism explains a pattern observed across multiple species; may include empirical validation in one or more taxa. Home journal: Nature / Science / PNAS.

Sweet Trap is **Type C** with supporting evidence from Types A and B.

## Confirmed Recent Examples in Corpus

### [1] Local convergence of behavior across species
- **Journal/Year:** *Science* 2021
- **DOI:** 10.1126/science.abb7481 (cites=106)
- **What they did:** Analyzed large-scale animal tracking data across multiple ecologically diverse species; demonstrated that animals with similar ecological roles (apex predators, grazers) converge on similar behavioral strategies despite phylogenetic distance.
- **Structure:** Cross-species data → convergence pattern → universal behavioral principle claim
- **How Sweet Trap matches:** We use: cross-species evidence catalog → convergence on reward-fitness decoupling → universal mechanism claim (Sweet Trap). Same logical structure. Their data was GPS tracking; ours is evolutionary theory + neural evidence + human panel data.
- **Key difference we exploit:** They describe behavioral convergence but do not offer a unified mechanistic model. Sweet Trap provides the formal model (θ, λ, β, ρ) that explains *why* the convergence occurs.

### [2] Cultural flies: Conformist social learning in fruitflies predicts long-lasting mate-choice tradition
- **Journal/Year:** *Science* 2018
- **DOI:** 10.1126/science.aat1590 (cites=223)
- **What they did:** Demonstrated that Drosophila melanogaster copy the mate choices of observed conspecifics, creating stable cultural traditions in mate preference — showing cultural transmission of preferences in an invertebrate.
- **Structure:** Behavioral experiment (insect) → discovery of "cultural" mechanism → implication for evolutionary universality of cultural transmission
- **How Sweet Trap matches:** Our ρ (lock-in) mechanism relies on conformist cultural transmission. This paper proves ρ operates even in insects, supporting universality.
- **Key difference we exploit:** They demonstrate the existence of cultural transmission in invertebrates; we show that cultural transmission can create evolutionarily stable welfare-reducing equilibria — a consequence they do not develop.

### [3] Irrationality in mate choice revealed by túngara frogs
- **Journal/Year:** *Science* 2015
- **DOI:** 10.1126/science.aab2012 (cites=127)
- **What they did:** Demonstrated context-dependent irrationality (decoy effects, preference reversal) in túngara frog mate choice — showing that systematic departures from utility maximization occur in amphibians.
- **Structure:** Behavioral experiment (frog) → demonstration of choice anomaly → claim that anomaly is evolutionarily conserved, not human-specific
- **How Sweet Trap matches:** Sweet Trap also claims that a systematic behavioral pattern (endorsing welfare-reducing choices when reward signals are activated) is evolutionarily conserved. We use the same "not human-specific" argument.
- **Key difference we exploit:** Túngara frogs show choice anomalies in a single domain (mate choice); Sweet Trap shows a unifying mechanism across all reward domains.

### [4] Lethal trap created by adaptive evolutionary response to an exotic resource
- **Journal/Year:** *Nature* 2018
- **DOI:** 10.1038/s41586-018-0074-6 (cites=102)
- **What they did:** Empirical study showing that adaptive evolutionary response to an introduced plant species created a population-level lethal trap for a native beetle: the beetles were attracted to and preferentially used the invasive plant, but this resource was lethal.
- **Structure:** Single species × novel environment → evolutionary trap → fitness consequence
- **How Sweet Trap matches:** This is the most direct Nature main precedent. It publishes "evolutionary trap" in Nature. Our scope is much larger: multiple species × multiple novel environments × universal formal mechanism × human welfare quantification.
- **Key difference we exploit:** Their paper is one species, one trap. We generalize the mechanism to a universal class. This is the "we supersede scope" argument.

### [5] Conserved brain-wide emergence of emotional response in humans and mice
- **Journal/Year:** *Science* 2025
- **DOI:** 10.1126/science.adt3971 (cites=18)
- **What they did:** Large-scale imaging showing that emotional responses to sensory stimuli are encoded by homologous brain circuits in humans and mice; the response patterns are conserved.
- **Structure:** Human + rodent neural data → conservation demonstration → evolutionary implication
- **How Sweet Trap matches:** Our neural mechanism argument (Olds-Milner through Berridge) rests on this conservation: if reward/emotional circuits are conserved, then reward-fitness decoupling should occur whenever those circuits are activated by novel stimuli in any species with the circuits.
- **Key difference we exploit:** They document conservation; we show the functional consequence of that conservation in novel environments (the Sweet Trap).

### [6] Humans share acoustic preferences with other animals
- **Journal/Year:** *Science* 2026
- **DOI:** 10.1126/science.aea1202 (cites=1)
- **What they did:** Comparative study showing that humans and non-human animals share specific preferences for acoustic features rooted in shared evolutionary history; cross-species preference convergence.
- **Structure:** Cross-species preference data → evolutionary origin argument → universality claim
- **How Sweet Trap matches:** Same "shared evolved preference" genre. Published in 2026, confirming the genre is active in the current submission window.
- **Key difference we exploit:** They document that shared preferences exist; we document the *maladaptive consequence* when those shared preferences are activated by evolutionarily novel stimuli.

### [7] Reducing nighttime light exposure in the urban environment to benefit human health and society
- **Journal/Year:** *Science* 2023
- **DOI:** 10.1126/science.adg5277 (cites=128)
- **What they did:** Comprehensive review showing that artificial light at night has adverse effects across multiple species (insects, birds, sea turtles) and humans (circadian disruption); proposes policy framework.
- **Structure:** Multi-species evidence (ecological trap literature) → human health evidence → unified policy recommendation
- **How Sweet Trap matches:** This is the closest existing paper to our multi-species + human evidence structure! They demonstrate the same mechanism (ancestrally calibrated light-response system = Sweet Trap) across multiple species. This is almost a Sweet Trap paper, just without the formal mechanism model, the endorsement condition, and the economic welfare analysis.
- **Key difference we exploit:** Swaddle/Francis et al. describe the ecological trap; we provide the formal mechanism (θ, λ, β, ρ), the endorsement condition (agents actively choose the stimulus), and quantify human welfare losses from primary data.

## Journal Selection Analysis

### Nature main

**Criteria for Nature main cross-species behavior papers:**
1. Novel mechanism or principle, not just novel findings
2. Multi-species scope or explicitly addresses universality
3. Clear welfare/relevance implication for humans or ecosystems
4. Combines empirical + theoretical
5. High visual/conceptual impact (good figures)

**Nature's sweet spot for our paper:** The intersection of evolutionary biology + behavioral science + human welfare. Nature has published:
- Neural conservation (multiple papers 2020-2025)
- Evolutionary trap (2018)
- Gene-culture coevolution themes (review articles)
- Behavioral economics applications to real-world welfare (Social Capital papers 2022)

**Assessment:** Nature main is achievable if we lead with the evolutionary biology framing (reward-fitness decoupling as universal evolutionary mechanism) and use the human data as validation rather than the centerpiece. The human panel data must be positioned as "the largest-scale cross-cultural test of the theory" not as "a Chinese survey study."

### Science

**Criteria for Science cross-species behavior papers:**
1. Clear evolutionary/biological mechanism
2. Cross-species comparative evidence
3. New principle, not incremental finding
4. Accessible to broad audience

**Science's sweet spot:** Comparative behavioral ecology, evolutionary mechanisms with broad implications, human-animal comparisons. Science has published more behavioral ecology (frogs 2015, flies 2018, species convergence 2021) than Nature. More receptive to comparative behavioral science without requiring massive neural datasets.

**Assessment:** Science is slightly higher probability than Nature main for our paper. The Science cross-species behavior genre (frogs, flies, convergence papers) exactly matches our framing. The risk: if our paper skews too heavily toward human behavioral economics (CFPS data), Science may desk reject as "not broad enough."

### Nature Ecology & Evolution (backup)

**Criteria:** Strong evolutionary biology framing, multi-species evidence, mechanistic model. Less requirement for human welfare angle.

**Assessment:** High fit probability (70-80%) if Nature main / Science decline. NE&E has published evolutionary trap extensions and mismatch theory applications. Our formal model + empirical validation is exactly their target.

### Nature Human Behaviour (original target, now backup)

**Assessment:** Remains viable if we cannot persuade the cross-species angle. But the upgrade to Nature main/Science is justified by the evolutionary biology framing — we should submit to NE&E before NHB if top-tier declines.

## Sweet Trap Submission Strategy

**First submission: Nature main**
- Frame as: "A universal evolutionary mechanism — reward-fitness decoupling — that generates self-reinforcing welfare-reducing equilibria across all species with ancestrally calibrated reward systems"
- Lead with non-human examples (moth, sea turtle, guppies, Drosophila)
- Position human data as "unprecedented multi-domain empirical test" (not just China study)
- Use the Science 2023 light-pollution paper as context: "prior work has documented the ecological consequences of specific traps; we provide the first formal mechanism that unifies all instances"

**If Nature desk rejects:**
- Second submission: Science
- Reframe as comparative behavioral science: "From moths to markets: a formal theory of reward-fitness decoupling across species"
- Emphasize the behavioral convergence genre (abb7481 as template)

**If Science desk rejects:**
- Third submission: Nature Ecology & Evolution
- Reframe as evolutionary ecology: focus more on the formal evolutionary model, reduce human panel data to supplementary

**Do NOT submit to NHB until all three above decline.** NHB is a step down from NE&E for a paper with strong evolutionary biology content.
