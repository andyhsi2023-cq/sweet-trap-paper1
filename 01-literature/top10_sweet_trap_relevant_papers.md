# Top 10 Sweet Trap Relevant Papers (Cross-Domain)
**Compiled:** 2026-04-17
**Ranking criteria:** (1) conceptual proximity to Sweet Trap = rational endorsement of welfare-reducing behavior, (2) methodological benchmark for NHB-level identification, (3) citation risk (must cite or get attacked in review)

---

## Rank 1 — Allcott et al. (2020, QJE)
**Allcott, H., Braghieri, L., Bursztyn, L., & Gentzkow, M. (2020).** The welfare effects of social media. *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 135(1), 211–254.

**What they found:** Facebook deactivation RCT (N=2,844). Deactivation improved wellbeing (+0.09 SD), reduced polarization, freed 60 min/day. But after study ended, almost all participants re-activated — demonstrating they chose to return to a platform that demonstrably harmed their wellbeing.

**This is the closest existing Sweet Trap paper.** The re-endorsement after experimentally demonstrated harm is exactly the "rational endorsement of welfare-reducing choice" pattern. They call it "subjective misallocation" but do not formalize it as a general mechanism.

**How we cite it:** "Our study formalizes and extends the 'subjective misallocation' documented by Allcott et al. (2020) into a four-primitive behavioral equilibrium — the Sweet Trap — and tests it across ≥5 human life domains rather than one platform."

**How we differentiate:** (1) General construct vs. single-platform finding. (2) Within-person longitudinal FE vs. one-time RCT. (3) Four-mechanism formalization vs. reduced-form welfare estimate. (4) Chinese context (algorithmic recommendation > organic social network).

## Rank 2 — Braghieri, Levy & Palomino (2022, AER)
**Braghieri, L., Levy, R. E., & Palomino, A. (2022).** Social media and mental health. *American Economic Review*, 112(11), 3660–3693.

**What they found:** Staggered Facebook rollout across US college campuses 2004-2006 as natural experiment. Mental health deteriorated: depression +7%, anxiety +20%, mental health index −0.3 SD. Causal identification via DiD on rollout timing.

**How we cite it:** Best causal identification of social media → mental health harm. Our Domain 7 replication extends to Chinese Douyin/short-video context using CFPS within-person FE over 2010-2022.

**How we differentiate:** Chinese algorithmic short-video vs. US organic social. CFPS multi-wave panel vs. college-cohort cross-sectional DiD.

---

## Rank 3 — Orben & Przybylski (2019, NHB)
**Orben, A., & Przybylski, A. K. (2019).** The association between adolescent well-being and digital technology use. *Nature Human Behaviour*, 3(2), 173–182.

**What they found:** Specification curve across 40,966 model variants using UK/US adolescent survey data. 89.4% of specifications are negative but effect sizes tiny (wearing glasses explains more variance than screen time). Conclude evidence is inconclusive.

**Why it matters:** This is the NHB flagship paper that many reviewers will cite to say "effect sizes are trivially small." We must address this directly.

**How we differentiate:** (1) Cross-sectional vs. within-person FE — SCA cannot control for stable individual traits. (2) Adolescent surveys vs. CFPS adult panel. (3) We measure endorsement trajectory (people who know harm still re-endorse), not just association magnitude. (4) Chinese Douyin algorithmic context has stronger engagement loops than generic screen time.

---

## Rank 4 — Laibson (1997, QJE)
**Laibson, D. (1997).** Golden eggs and hyperbolic discounting. *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 112(2), 443–477.

**What they found:** β-δ quasi-hyperbolic discounting formalization; golden-egg commitment device problem; shows present-biased agents make systematically wrong intertemporal choices. Foundational for β mechanism in Sweet Trap.

**How we cite it:** "The temporal asymmetry in the Sweet Trap — immediate amenity benefit, deferred cost — is a direct application of Laibson's (1997) quasi-hyperbolic discounting. Our contribution is to show that this structure operates at equilibrium across ≥5 human life domains, not only savings decisions."

---

## Rank 5 — Bernheim & Taubinsky (2018, HBE)
**Bernheim, B. D., & Taubinsky, D. (2018).** Behavioral public economics. *Handbook of Behavioral Economics*, 1, 381–516.

**What they found:** Defines internalities = welfare costs individuals impose on themselves due to behavioral failures. Provides welfare analysis framework for paternalistic policy interventions. Distinguishes "as-if rational" from "fully rational" welfare analysis.

**How we cite it:** Sweet Trap is a specific structural type of internality: not a bias (the agent is locally rational) but a behavioral equilibrium with externalization. We claim this goes beyond the internality framework.

**How we differentiate:** Bernheim-Taubinsky focus on deviations from rational benchmark; Sweet Trap shows the rational benchmark itself is welfare-reducing when θ + λ + β + ρ are jointly activated. Policy implication differs: internality → nudge/tax; Sweet Trap → structural change to cost timing.

---

## Rank 6 — Goldin (2014, AER)
**Goldin, C. (2014).** A grand gender convergence: Its last chapter. *American Economic Review*, 104(4), 1091–1119.

**What they found:** "Greedy jobs" — nonlinear pay schedule rewards overwork disproportionately; individually rational for workers to over-supply labor; creates gender gap because women bear childcare costs.

**How we cite it:** Domain 3 (overwork) key benchmark. Contains ρ (once career norms set at overwork, deviation is costly) and β (immediate career reward over-weighted vs. deferred health/family cost). Sweet Trap framing makes the welfare distortion explicit.

**How we differentiate:** Goldin focuses on gender inequality; Sweet Trap focuses on within-person endorsement-harm paradox regardless of gender. We want to show men who work 996 report highest career satisfaction AND worst family outcomes simultaneously.

---

## Rank 7 — Becker & Murphy (1988, JPE)
**Becker, G. S., & Murphy, K. M. (1988).** A theory of rational addiction. *Journal of Political Economy*, 96(4), 675–700.

**What they found:** Rational agents can optimally choose addictive behaviors given correct intertemporal preferences; addiction is not irrational per se. Present-discounted utility maximization with habit formation.

**How we cite it:** Mandatory cite; Sweet Trap is adjacent. We differentiate explicitly: "Unlike rational addiction (Becker & Murphy 1988), where agents correctly anticipate the habit formation cost and still optimize, the Sweet Trap involves rational endorsement that is systematically biased by externalization (λ) — costs falling on others — making individually rational choices collectively suboptimal."

---

## Rank 8 — Kivimäki et al. (2015, Lancet)
**Kivimäki, M., et al. (2015).** Long working hours and risk of coronary heart disease and stroke: A systematic review and meta-analysis of published and unpublished data in 603,838 individuals. *Lancet*, 386(10005), 1739–1746.

**What they found:** Working ≥55 h/week vs. standard hours: CVD risk +13%, stroke risk +33% (dose-response). N=603,838.

**How we cite it:** Domain 3 anchor for λ quantification. Health cost externalized from current-work-self to future-sick-self. "The welfare cost of overwork is not hypothetical: Kivimäki et al. (2015) document a dose-response relationship between working hours and stroke risk at population scale."

**How we differentiate:** They document harm; we document the paradox that workers simultaneously report career satisfaction and accumulate these costs.

---

## Rank 9 — Luttmer (2005, QJE)
**Luttmer, E. F. P. (2005).** Neighbors as negatives: Relative income and well-being. *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 120(3), 963–1002.

**What they found:** N=30K NSFH; neighbors' income negatively affects own wellbeing conditional on own income. Reference dependence / positional goods mechanism empirically confirmed.

**How we cite it:** Domain 8 (housing/vehicle) foundation. θ mechanism: consumption utility is partly positional, so over-investment in status goods generates immediate satisfaction increase but deferred position-maintenance treadmill. "Luttmer (2005) documents the positional income externality; our Sweet Trap formalizes this as a θ-amplified endorsement equilibrium."

---

## Rank 10 — Fiolet et al. (2018, BMJ) / Hall et al. (2019, Cell Metabolism)
**Fiolet, T., et al. (2018).** Consumption of ultra-processed foods and cancer risk. *BMJ*, 360, k322. N=104,980.
**Hall, K. D., et al. (2019).** Ultra-processed diets cause excess calorie intake and weight gain. *Cell Metabolism*, 30(1), 67–77. RCT N=20.

**What they found:** UPF consumption → cancer risk (Fiolet epidemiology); UPF ad libitum RCT → +508 kcal/day, weight gain (Hall causal).

**How we cite it:** Domain 5 harm-side anchor. The causal case for UPF → health harm is established. Our increment: within-person CFPS evidence of endorsement despite harm, and formal β-parameterization of the food choice Sweet Trap.
