# Top 20 Cross-Species Benchmarks for Sweet Trap
**Stage:** 0γ  
**Compiled:** 2026-04-17  
**Purpose:** Ranked list of the 20 most important cross-species papers for the Sweet Trap Nature/Science submission

Ranking criteria: (1) direct relevance to reward-fitness decoupling across species, (2) publication venue quality, (3) citation count as proxy for field influence, (4) recency for target journal referees.

---

## Tier 1: Must Cite (Nature/Science Genre Setters)

**[1] Robertson, B. A., Rehage, J. S., & Sih, A. (2013).** Ecological novelty and the emergence of evolutionary traps. *Trends in Ecology & Evolution*, 28(9), 552–560.
- DOI: 10.1016/j.tree.2013.04.004
- **Relevance:** Nearest construct to Sweet Trap. Defines evolutionary trap as the field-standard concept we must position against. Any Nature reviewer who knows this field will ask "how is this different from Robertson 2013?" One-sentence answer: Sweet Trap additionally requires endorsement, formal model, and self-reinforcing equilibrium.

**[2] Schlaepfer, M. A., Runge, M. C., & Sherman, P. W. (2002).** Ecological and evolutionary traps. *Trends in Ecology & Evolution*, 17(10), 474–480.
- DOI: 10.1016/S0169-5347(02)02580-6
- **Relevance:** The founding paper of the ecological trap literature. Over 20 years of field-building. We explicitly cite this as the habitat-level special case that Sweet Trap generalizes.

**[3] Olds, J., & Milner, P. (1954).** Positive reinforcement produced by electrical stimulation of septal area and other regions of rat brain. *Journal of Comparative and Physiological Psychology*, 47(6), 419–427.
- DOI: 10.1037/h0058775
- **Relevance:** First empirical demonstration of reward-fitness decoupling in a mammal (rat self-stimulation). Our animal model origin story. Foundational enough to be cited in page 1 of the paper.

**[4] Fisher, R. A. (1930).** *The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection.* Clarendon Press.
- DOI: [VERIFY — book]
- **Relevance:** Runaway selection is the evolutionary biology prototype of Sweet Trap: a reward signal (mate preference) evolving into a self-reinforcing trap (peacock tail excess). Every evolutionary biologist reviewer will recognize this as an ancestor of our framework.

**[5] Nesse, R. M., & Williams, G. C. (1994).** *Why We Get Sick.* Times Books.
- DOI: [VERIFY — book]
- **Relevance:** Mismatch theory founding text. We position Sweet Trap as mismatch theory + formal model + endorsement mechanism + cross-domain unification.

**[6] Sih, A., Ferrari, M. C. O., & Harris, D. J. (2011).** Evolution and behavioural responses to HIREC. *Evolutionary Applications*, 4(2), 367–387.
- DOI: 10.1111/j.1752-4571.2010.00166.x
- **Relevance:** HIREC framework. Reviews cross-species maladaptive responses to rapid human-caused change. Establishes the scope of the problem across taxa.

**[7] Nature 2018 — Lethal trap from adaptive evolutionary response to exotic resource.** DOI: 10.1038/s41586-018-0074-6 (cites=102).
- **Relevance:** Only Nature main paper in corpus directly documenting evolutionary trap in non-human species. Sets the genre ceiling that we need to exceed in scope and generality.

**[8] Science 2021 — Local convergence of behavior across species.** DOI: 10.1126/science.abb7481 (cites=106).
- **Relevance:** Benchmark for "cross-species behavioral convergence" papers in Science. Same genre as our paper. Methods template.

## Tier 2: High Relevance (Mechanism and Scope Builders)

**[9] Berridge, K. C., & Robinson, T. E. (1998).** What is the role of dopamine in reward. *Brain Research Reviews*, 28(3), 309–369.
- DOI: 10.1016/S0165-0173(98)00019-8
- **Relevance:** Wanting/liking dissociation is the neural mechanism of our endorsement-without-payoff feature. Essential for the neuroscience audience.

**[10] Swaddle, J. P., et al. (2015).** A framework to assess evolutionary responses to anthropogenic light and sound. *Trends in Ecology & Evolution*, 30(2), 67–76.
- DOI: 10.1016/j.tree.2014.11.001
- **Relevance:** Cross-species review of anthropogenic sensory traps (50+ species). Provides our empirical backbone for the non-human examples section.

**[11] McClure, S. M., et al. (2004).** Separate neural systems value immediate and delayed monetary rewards. *Science*, 306(5695), 503–505.
- DOI: 10.1126/science.1100907
- **Relevance:** Neural architecture of present bias. β parameter mechanistic foundation.

**[12] Science 2018 — Cultural flies.** DOI: 10.1126/science.aat1590 (cites=223).
- **Relevance:** Conformist social learning (our ρ mechanism) demonstrated in Drosophila. Shows cultural lock-in is cross-species, even in invertebrates.

**[13] Science 2015 — Irrationality in mate choice in túngara frogs.** DOI: 10.1126/science.aab2012 (cites=127).
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates systematic choice anomalies (context effects, preference reversal) in amphibians. Supports our claim that reward signal decoupling is cross-species.

**[14] Nature 2023 — Dopaminergic systems create reward seeking despite adverse consequences.** DOI: 10.1038/s41586-023-06671-8 (cites=24).
- **Relevance:** Direct neural circuit evidence for adverse-consequence reward seeking. The most recent Nature paper directly supporting our mechanism.

**[15] Science 2025 — Conserved brain-wide emotional response in humans and mice.** DOI: 10.1126/science.adt3971 (cites=18).
- **Relevance:** Neural conservation of emotional/reward circuitry across mammals. Supports cross-species mechanism claim.

**[16] Zahavi, A. (1975).** Mate selection — a selection for a handicap. *Journal of Theoretical Biology*, 53(1), 205–214.
- DOI: 10.1016/0022-5193(75)90111-3
- **Relevance:** Handicap principle explains why costly reward signals are evolutionarily stable, setting up the environment-change perturbation that creates Sweet Trap.

## Tier 3: Supporting Evidence

**[17] Maynard Smith, J. (1982).** *Evolution and the Theory of Games.*
- **Relevance:** ESS framework underpins our group-level lock-in model. Formally validates that Sweet Trap equilibria can be evolutionarily stable even when collectively suboptimal.

**[18] Science 2026 — Humans share acoustic preferences with other animals.** DOI: 10.1126/science.aea1202 (cites=1).
- **Relevance:** Most recent (2026) cross-species shared preference paper in Science. Confirms genre viability for current submission cycle.

**[19] Science 2023 — Reducing nighttime light exposure: human health and society.** DOI: 10.1126/science.adg5277 (cites=128).
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates same stimulus (artificial light) creating traps across species AND humans. Multi-species, one-mechanism paper exactly in our genre.

**[20] Nature 2019 — Evolutionarily stable strategy to colonize extended habitats.** DOI: 10.1038/s41586-019-1734-x (cites=72).
- **Relevance:** ESS colonization dynamics; shows that evolutionary stable strategies can involve persistent suboptimal habitat use. Supports the formal evolutionary stability of Sweet Trap equilibria.
