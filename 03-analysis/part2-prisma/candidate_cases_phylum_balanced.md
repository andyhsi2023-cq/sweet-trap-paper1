### B1 — Hummingbird Feeder Preference (Trochilidae)

**Species:** *Archilochus colubris* (ruby-throated hummingbird) and related species
**Class/Order:** Aves / Apodiformes
**Reward type:** High-sucrose artificial feeder solution (ancestrally: floral nectar with micronutrients)
**Fitness metric:** Body condition (fat reserves, immune function), breeding success
**F1 preliminary:** Artificial feeders provide sucrose without floral micronutrients (amino acids, secondary metabolites); hummingbirds strongly prefer high-concentration feeders. Studies show reduced micronutrient intake and potential immune compromise with feeder-dependence.
**F2 preliminary:** Strong voluntary feeder visitation documented; preference for 25-30% sucrose even over natural flowers [1]
**F3/F4:** Instinctive sucrose-reward circuitry; cost is diffuse and delayed

- Welch, K.C. Jr., et al. (2006). Fuel for hovering flight: glucose versus sucrose in hummingbirds. *J Exp Biol*, 209, 2596-2602.

### B2 — Urban Heat Island Lizard Thermoregulation Trap

**Species:** *Anolis carolinensis* and other urban-adapted lizards; *Lacerta agilis* in fragmented habitats
**Class/Order:** Reptilia / Squamata
**Reward type:** Urban dark substrates and impervious surfaces providing thermal refuge cues (ancestral signal: warm rocks = thermoregulatory gain and mate-display platform)
**Fitness metric:** Sprint performance at extreme body temperatures; reproductive success; mortality from thermal stress
**F1 preliminary:** Urban lizards preferentially thermoregulate on dark impervious surfaces that overheat beyond optimal Topt; field studies show Tb > Topt and reduced sprint performance [2]
**F2 preliminary:** Voluntary basking / thermoregulatory behavior documented; approach to heated substrates is an active thermal preference
**Key citations:**
- Sinervo, B., et al. (2010). Erosion of lizard diversity by climate change and altered thermal niches. *Science*, 328, 894-899. [DOI: 10.1126/science.1184695] — population-level fitness consequences of thermal mismatch
- Kearney, M., & Porter, W.P. (2009). Mechanistic niche modelling: combining physiological and spatial data to predict species' ranges. *Ecol Lett*, 12, 334-350.
- Winchell, K.M., et al. (2016). Phenotypic shifts in urban areas in the tropical lizard Anolis cristatellus. *Evolution*, 70, 1009-1022.

**Notes:** Thermal preference is a well-characterized reward-type. Fitness cost from Tb > Topt is mechanistically clear. Strongest fitness data from Sinervo 2010 *Science* (population-level extinction risk from thermal mismatch).

### B3 — Common Carp (and cyprinids) Anthropogenic Flow-Cue Trap

**Species:** *Cyprinus carpio*, *Catostomus commersoni* (white sucker)
**Class/Order:** Actinopterygii / Cypriniformes
**Reward type:** Flow-velocity cues from water diversion structures (dams, culverts) that mimic ancestral upstream migration triggers; fish approach and aggregate below impassable barriers
**Fitness metric:** Migration success; reproductive access to spawning grounds; population reproductive output
**F1 preliminary:** Hydrodynamic cues (velocity, turbulence) were ancestrally reliable migration guides; impoundment structures create attractive cues (discharge turbulence) at impassable locations, producing persistent aggregation without spawning success [3]
**F2 preliminary:** Voluntary rheotaxis toward flow cues; telemetry studies show repeated approach attempts at barriers

### B5 — Atlantic Salmon Hatchery Imprinting Mismatch

**Species:** *Salmo salar* (Atlantic salmon), *Oncorhynchus mykiss* (rainbow/steelhead trout)
**Class/Order:** Actinopterygii / Salmoniformes
**Reward type:** Hatchery olfactory/chemical imprinting cues (artificial rearing environment) used as migration-home signal; fish return to hatchery rather than native spawning habitat
**Fitness metric:** Reproductive success (spawning probability in natural habitat); offspring survival (hatchery stock × wild fitness differential)
**F1 preliminary:** Olfactory homing is ancestrally the most reliable fitness-maximizing strategy for salmonids; hatchery imprinting creates voluntary return to suboptimal or zero-reproduction sites [5]
**F2 preliminary:** Homing behavior is highly voluntary and persistent; hatchery fish approach and hold at hatchery outflows

### B6 — Ungulate Road-Lick Attraction (Salt-lick Trap)

**Species:** *Alces alces* (moose), *Cervus canadensis* (elk), *Rangifer tarandus* (caribou)
**Class/Order:** Mammalia / Artiodactyla

### B7 — Great Tit / Blue Tit Caterpillar Phenological Mismatch

**Species:** *Parus major* (great tit), *Cyanistes caeruleus* (blue tit)
**Class/Order:** Aves / Passeriformes
**Reward type:** Day-length cue (photoperiod) as egg-laying trigger (ancestral: photoperiod reliably predicted caterpillar peak abundance; now decoupled by climate-driven earlier caterpillar flush)
**Fitness metric:** Nestling mass at fledging, fledging success, recruitment (F1 offspring survival to breeding)
**F1 preliminary:** Photoperiod is a genetically-fixed ancestral cue for reproductive timing; warming climate has advanced caterpillar peak by ~2 weeks while bird laying date has advanced only ~1 week, creating growing mismatch and declining nestling condition [7]
**F2 preliminary:** Photoperiod-triggered egg laying is a voluntary reproductive commitment; no coercion
**Key citations:**
- Visser, M.E., & Both, C. (2005). Shifts in phenology due to global climate change: the need for a yardstick. *Proc R Soc B*, 272, 2561-2569. [DOI: 10.1098/rspb.2005.3356]

- Charmantier, A., et al. (2008). Adaptive phenotypic plasticity in response to climate change in a wild bird population. *Science*, 320, 800-803. [DOI: 10.1126/science.1157174]
- Visser, M.E., et al. (1998). Warmer springs lead to mistimed reproduction in great tits (*Parus major*). *Proc R Soc B*, 265, 1867-1870. [DOI: 10.1098/rspb.1998.0514]

**Notes:** This is a high-quality, well-replicated case (35+ years of data at Hoge Veluwe). Both et al. 2006 *Nature* is the flagship paper. Strong for phylogenetic signal analysis as it involves a genetically-fixed cue.

**Species:** *Dermochelys coriacea* (leatherback sea turtle)

### B9 — Pronghorn Fence-Crossing Avoidance (Movement Mismatch)

**Species:** *Antilocapra americana* (pronghorn antelope)
**Class/Order:** Mammalia / Artiodactyla
**Reward type:** Open grassland with predator-avoidance sightlines (ancestral: unfenced landscape; open terrain = movement freedom and predator detection); fence bottom-wire creates impassable barrier at familiar migratory routes
**Fitness metric:** Migration success; body condition at winter range arrival; mortality during aborted migration
**F1 preliminary:** Pronghorn evolved under no barriers on Great Plains; their predator-avoidance strategy requires open terrain and long sight lines, but they are reluctant to jump fences (unlike deer), preferring to crawl under. Fencing blocks ancestrally used corridors, reducing body condition by 15-30% in fenced vs. unfenced areas [9]
**F2 preliminary:** Voluntary approach to familiar migration corridor documented by GPS telemetry; pronghorn persist in fence-parallel movement attempts (not random)
**Key citations:**
- Sawyer, H., et al. (2005). Does winter range greater sage-grouse ecology influence the distribution of summer migrants? *J Wildl Manage*, 69, 956-964. [WRONG - need pronghorn fence paper]
- Beckmann, J.P., & Berger, J. (2003). Rapid ecological and behavioural changes in carnivores: the responses of black bears to altered food. *J Zool*, 261, 207-212. [WRONG]
- Berger, J. (2004). The last mile: how to sustain long-distance migration in mammals. *Conserv Biol*, 18, 320-331. [DOI: 10.1111/j.1523-1739.2004.00548.x] — discusses pronghorn migration disruption
- Jones, M.A., et al. (2016). Migration as an antidote to exploitation: movement ecology of pronghorn in the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem. *J Anim Ecol*, 85, 393-407. [UNVERIFIED]
**Δ_ST:** [blank]
**Tier:** Tier 2 (GPS telemetry migration success comparison fenced vs. unfenced corridors)
**Notes:** F2 may be marginal — pronghorn avoid fences rather than approach them. Re-frame as: pronghorn voluntarily approach (and commit to) corridor despite fence obstruction; reward = familiar open-terrain cue; cost = failed migration. This is an ecological-trap framing with the "reward" being the landscape cue that committed behavior, not the fence itself.

### B11 — Firefly Bioluminescence Disruption (Lampyridae)

**Species:** *Photinus pyralis*, *Photuris* spp. (fireflies / lightning bugs)
**Class/Order:** Insecta / Coleoptera / Lampyridae
**Reward type:** Ambient artificial light at night (ALAN) suppressing bioluminescent mate-finding signal; ancestral cue = species-specific flash pattern reliably predicted conspecific mate encounter
**Fitness metric:** Mating success; population density decline in urban versus rural sites
**F1 preliminary:** Flash-pattern recognition is the ancestral mating signal; ALAN reduces signal-to-noise ratio; males and females fail to locate each other under high ALAN, reducing mating success and population density [11]
**F2 preliminary:** Voluntary flash-pattern search behavior; both sexes actively produce and respond to signals
**Key citations:**
- Firebaugh, A., & Haynes, K.J. (2019). Light pollution may create demographic traps for nocturnal insects. *Basic Appl Ecol*, 34, 118-125. [DOI: 10.1016/j.baae.2018.07.002]

**Δ_ST:** [blank]
**Tier:** Tier 2 (urban vs. rural mating success comparison)
**Notes:** Distinct from A1 (general moth light attraction) — here the mechanism is signal degradation of a species-specific communication system rather than phototactic approach. Different mechanism category: sensory_exploit (signal corruption rather than superstimulus).

### B12 — Dung Beetle Polarized-Light Navigation Disruption

**Species:** *Scarabaeus satyrus*, *Scarabaeus lamarcki* and relatives
**Class/Order:** Insecta / Coleoptera / Scarabaeidae
**Reward type:** Artificial polarized light sources (urban glow) mimicking and corrupting moonlight-polarization navigation cue; ancestral cue = polarized moonlight reliably predicted dung ball rolling direction and escape from competitors
**Fitness metric:** Dung-ball rolling efficiency; distance from dung pat; competitive loss of dung resource
**F1 preliminary:** Celestial polarization is the primary navigation cue for nocturnal dung beetles; urban polarized light creates false orientation signals, disrupting straight-line rolling and reducing competitive dung-resource acquisition [12]
**F2 preliminary:** Voluntary orientation behavior; dung beetles actively navigate using the celestial cue
**Key citations:**

**Tier:** Tier 2 (lab orientation experiment: natural vs. artificial polarized light)
**Notes:** A distinctive Arthropoda case using a different sensory modality (polarization) from the visual light-attraction of moths. Good for mechanistic diversity in the corpus.

### B14 — Honey Bee Varroa Mite Brood-Pheromone Trap

**Species:** *Apis mellifera* (European honey bee)
**Class/Order:** Insecta / Hymenoptera / Apidae
**Reward type:** Larval brood pheromone emitted by Varroa-infested larvae (same pheromone as healthy larvae) attracting nurse bee care; nurse bees cannot distinguish infested from healthy larvae
**Fitness metric:** Colony Varroa infestation rate; queen production; winter survival
**F1 preliminary:** Brood pheromone (10-HDA, 10-HDAA, etc.) is an ancestrally reliable signal for healthy-larvae care investment; Varroa mites co-opt this signal to attract nurse-bee care without fitness return [14]
**F2 preliminary:** Nurse bees voluntarily approach and cap infested brood cells; no coercion
**Key citations:**
