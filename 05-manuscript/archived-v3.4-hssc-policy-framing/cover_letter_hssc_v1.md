**To the Editor**
*Humanities and Social Sciences Communications*

Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, **"Sweet Trap: a cross-disciplinary framework for reward–fitness decoupling and its implications for behavioural policy"**, for consideration as an Article in *Humanities and Social Sciences Communications*. The work sits deliberately at the intersection of evolutionary behavioural science, behavioural economics, and contemporary public policy — three registers that have long circled the same phenomenon without a shared formal object — and argues that a single cross-disciplinary construct can unify them with direct empirical and policy purchase.

**What we contribute.** We formalise an axiomatic framework of reward–fitness decoupling (**Sweet Trap**: four axioms, four theorems, one keystone scalar Δ_ST = *U*_perc − 𝔼[*U*_fit ∣ *B*]) and validate its core claims across a four-layer empirical programme spanning non-human biology, human social surveys, molecular genetics, and the public intervention-effect literature: 20 animal cases across seven taxonomic classes; five human focal domains with 3,000 pre-registered specifications; 25 ISSP countries (*n* = 2.9 million individuals, 17 waves, 1985–2022); and 19 Mendelian-randomisation chains against FinnGen R12. Our central new theoretical result — **Theorem T2 (Intervention Asymmetry)** — proves that signal-redesign interventions structurally dominate information interventions by a factor of at least (1 − *w*_max)/*w*_max ≥ 1.5 under dose-matched comparison within Sweet Trap scope. This is a theorem, not an empirical generalisation; but its central prediction holds across six focal domains, with unit-matched ratios 74× (C8 retirement saving) and 7× (C12 short-video) clearing the theoretical floor by large margins, and signal-redesign dominating information in 6/6 domains. An F1+F2 binary classifier validates at Cohen's κ = 1.00 on 18 blind-coded cases spanning positive controls and systematic negatives.

**Why HSSC, and why now.** The framework explicitly bridges social-theory traditions that have named parts of the phenomenon in isolation — Veblen on conspicuous consumption, Schor on the work-and-spend cycle, Frank on positional externalities, Sen on commodity-command versus functioning, Zuboff on surveillance-capital instrumentation — and makes their shared intuition *empirically testable* through Δ_ST and T2. Three concurrent policy streams converge on the mechanisms we formalise. The WHO 2024 ultra-processed-food review and the UK sugar-tax decadal evaluation (2024–2025) are the first population-scale tests of signal-distribution rather than information-based health policy. The EU Digital Services Act (2024–) mandates algorithmic-recommender transparency without a mechanism-level framework for variable-ratio welfare losses. China's 2024 telecom-fraud crackdown (437,000 cases in 2023) and the FBI IC3's USD 4.57 billion confidence-fraud statistics describe an engineered-deception category that classical frameworks do not cleanly classify. Our axiomatic construct supplies the unifying architectural account and the testable intervention-asymmetry theorem that these policy conversations currently lack. This cross-disciplinary bridging work is precisely what HSSC was created to publish, and we believe the manuscript fits the journal's scope and readership exceptionally well.

**Methodology and pre-registration.** Our primary inferential evidence is the pre-registered A+D cross-level joint test (β = +1.58, *p* = 0.019), registered on 2026-04-17 (`cross_level_plan.md`) under a hierarchical decision rule before analysis. Discriminant validity was established through blind double-coding of 18 cases (binary κ = 1.00). All specifications, data harmonisation scripts, and the full text of the pre-registration are deposited at OSF (see below); we flag residual limitations explicitly in Discussion rather than in this letter.

**Reproducibility.** All analytical scripts, the 19-chain MR summary, the cross-level effect table, the intervention-asymmetry compilation, Paper 1 theory documents (axioms, theorems, lemmas), and Paper 2 Stage 3 findings are deposited at OSF `https://osf.io/ucxv7/` (public, deposited 2026-04-18, 23 files). Restricted-access data (CFPS, CHARLS, CHFS, UK Biobank, FinnGen R12) are accessed under respective institutional approvals; aggregated tables reproducing every figure are in the OSF deposit. The OSF deposit and GitHub repository (`sweet-trap-multidomain`) will be linked at publication.
