# Abstract
**Background.** Reward-fitness decoupling — systematic pursuit of proximate rewards that reduce realized fitness — has been described piecewise across animal taxa as evolutionary traps, ecological traps, sensory exploitation, and supernormal stimuli. Whether these phenomena reflect a *universal feature* of animal reward systems or a heterogeneous set of *lineage-specific outcomes* has not been tested at cross-phylum scale.

**Question.** We pre-registered three sharp predictions of universality: (i) cross-phylum phylogenetic signal in $\Delta_{ST}$ with Blomberg's $K > 0.30$; (ii) shared molecular substrate across phyla in reward-receptor families (Pfam Jaccard significantly above null); (iii) detectable positive selection on reward receptors across multiple ecological-shift lineages. We tested all three with a single integrated dataset.

**Approach.** A PRISMA-screened corpus of 114 cross-species cases of reward-fitness decoupling spanning 7 phyla and 56 species was assembled and scored on four operational criteria (F1–F4) and a continuous $\Delta_{ST}$ proxy. Time-calibrated phylogenetic signal was computed on TimeTree 5 with `phytools::phylosig` (9,999 permutations). Molecular substrate sharing was tested by Pfam Jaccard similarity between vertebrate TAS1R and insect Gr sweet-receptor families against a matched-random Pfam null baseline. Lineage-specific positive selection was probed by 21 PAML codeml branch-site tests on sweet-taste receptors across 6 production clades, anchored by the @baldwin2014hummingbird hummingbird TAS1R1 positive control.

**Results.** All three universality predictions are not supported. (i) $K = 0.117$ ($p = 0.251$), failing the pre-registered $K > 0.30$ threshold. (ii) Jaccard = 0 between TAS1R and Gr Pfam sets is *exactly the modal outcome* under the null baseline ($P(\text{Jaccard} = 0 \mid \text{null}) = 0.9998$); the data show non-shared, not shared, molecular substrate. (iii) After optimiser-boundary diagnostic on all 6 production clades [@anisimova2007branchsite], the matrix resolves as: 3/6 robustly null, **3/6 optimiser-sensitive (*Apis* Gr_sweet, dmel Gr64 cluster, dmel all-Grs — all show boundary-escape behaviour with foreground $\omega$ in the small-clade boundary-artefact range, $\omega > 30$ on $n \le 7$ taxa); 0/6 robust positives.** The hummingbird TAS1R1 positive control reproduces at LRT = 55.9 ($p < 4 \times 10^{-14}$), confirming pipeline integrity. The same dataset confirms widespread *existence*: 114 cases / 7 phyla / 56 species satisfy F1–F4. Within Arthropoda alone, $K = 1.446$ ($p = 0.007$) shows that signal can be detected where ecological exposure (ALAN phototaxis) clusters by clade. Four trans-ancestry Mendelian-randomization chains in humans confirm reward-fitness decoupling as a detectable phenomenon at the *Homo sapiens* tip (meta OR 1.12–1.41; all $p < 10^{-3}$).

**Conclusion.** Sweet Trap, defined as $\Delta_{ST} = U_\mathrm{perc} - \mathbb{E}[U_\mathrm{fit} \mid B]$, is a *widespread but not universal* phenomenon. Reward-fitness decoupling occurs in many animal lineages but does not behave as a phylogenetically inherited trait, does not rest on a shared molecular substrate across distant phyla, and does not generate robust positive-selection signal distinguishable from optimiser-boundary artefacts in any tested clade after diagnostic interrogation. We interpret these results as supporting a *lineage-specific origins* model in which reward-fitness decoupling re-emerges wherever local ecological conditions decouple proximate signals from realized fitness. A roadmap for identifying triggering conditions is presented for Paper 2.

**Keywords.** reward-fitness decoupling; evolutionary trap; pre-registered falsification; phylogenetic signal; lineage-specific origins; reward-receptor evolution.

# 1 Introduction
Every summer night, billions of flying insects spiral to their deaths around streetlamps [@fabian2024light]. Every year, more than half of sampled sea turtles are found with plastic in their guts, preferentially ingested because its olfactory signature mimics prey [@santos2021plastic]. In the twenty-first century, ultra-processed foods and algorithmically optimised digital feeds now account for the largest share of preventable mortality in high-income populations [@gbd2021risk; @Monteiro2018_NOVA]. These phenomena share a common structure: an organism repeatedly acts on a proximate reward signal, and the act reduces rather than promotes its fitness. Biologists have described this structure piecewise as *ecological trap* [@schlaepfer2002ecological], *evolutionary trap* [@robertson2013ecological], *sensory exploitation* [@ryan2013perceptual], *supernormal stimulus* [@tinbergen1951study], or *evolutionary mismatch* [@nesse2005natural; @livanvugt2018mismatch]. Each literature captures an aspect of reward-fitness decoupling but none supplies a common operational measure or a test of its evolutionary status.

Three competing readings of this phenomenon have circulated in parallel literatures. The first treats reward-fitness decoupling as a *phylogenetically inherited* susceptibility — a feature of an ancestral reward-system architecture preserved by descent. The second treats it as the visible footprint of a *deeply conserved* receptor machinery shared across animals. The third treats it as repeatedly *convergent*, re-derived in lineages whose ecology decouples proximate signals from fitness, regardless of phylogeny [@stern2013genetic; @losos2011convergence; @speedarbuckle2017convergence]. The three readings differ in their commitment to one underlying meta-question: is reward-fitness decoupling a *universal feature* of animal reward systems, or a heterogeneous set of *lineage-specific outcomes* that recur wherever local ecology decouples reward from fitness?

We name this shared phenomenon **Sweet Trap** and provide an operational scalar
$$
\Delta_{ST} = U_\mathrm{perc} - \mathbb{E}[U_\mathrm{fit} \mid B]
$$
where $U_\mathrm{perc}$ is the proximate reward signal received by the agent and $\mathbb{E}[U_\mathrm{fit} \mid B]$ is the fitness expectation given knowledge state $B$. Critically, *we do not assert that Sweet Trap is universal*. We pre-register and test the universal-version hypothesis. Sweet Trap is the *target* of our test, not its conclusion. This is the Popperian commitment that organises the present paper [@popper1959logic; @popper1963conjectures; @munafo2017manifesto]: a hypothesis worth defending must specify in advance the data that would refute it, and the value of the resulting paper lies in whether those refutation conditions are met.

We pre-register three falsifiable predictions of universality and test all three with a single integrated dataset. **H_universal_1** (phylogenetic signal): if Sweet Trap is a Metazoan-wide trait inherited by descent, $\Delta_{ST}$ should carry cross-phylum phylogenetic signal at Blomberg's $K > 0.30$ with prior probability $\ge 0.92$, derived from axioms A1–A4 (§2.1; Supplementary Note S1). **H_universal_2** (shared molecular substrate): if reward-receptor architecture is universal across phyla, Pfam Jaccard similarity between vertebrate TAS1R and insect Gr sweet-receptor families should exceed a matched-random Pfam null baseline. **H_universal_3** (cross-clade positive selection): if reward-receptor selection is universal across ecological-shift lineages, multiple production clades should show PAML branch-site LRT exceeding the pre-registered Bonferroni threshold. Each prediction has a quantitative threshold, an explicit falsification band, and a single-dataset test. None is post-hoc.

The scope of Paper 1 is specified up front. Paper 1 tests universality. If any of the three predictions fails, the universal-version hypothesis loses that pillar; if all three fail, the universal version is refuted in its strong form, and a *lineage-specific origins* model becomes the most parsimonious reading of the same data. Paper 2 will identify the lineage-specific conditions under which Sweet Trap emerges (severity of ecological niche shift, HIREC pressure, generation time, gene-family pre-conditions) and test those conditions on an independent case corpus. To establish priority on the research programme and to pre-register all analytical choices, we deposited a companion priority preprint on bioRxiv (BIORXIV/2026/720498, 2026-04-24) and a public OSF project (<https://osf.io/pv3ch/>; API endpoint <https://api.osf.io/v2/nodes/pv3ch/>) concurrent with data extraction. The OSF deposit (`sweet_trap_v4_stage6_preregistration.zip`, 168 KB, 19 stage6 documents) contains the three universality predictions tested in this manuscript verbatim: H3 Blomberg $K > 0.30$ with $\ge 80$% posterior probability under A1–A4 (`bioRxiv_priority_preprint_draft.md` + `path_B_formal_model_H3.md`); H4b cross-phylum Jaccard $\ge 0.70$ vs matched-random baseline $\le 0.30$ (`bioRxiv_priority_preprint_draft.md` + `evidence_architecture_v4.md`); H6a $\ge 1$ positive-selected gene on $\ge 1$ lineage at branch-site Bonferroni-corrected significance (`gantt_12_weeks.md` Gate-1 row). All deviations from this protocol are flagged below.

# 2 Methods
## 2.1 Framework: axioms, operational scalar, and coding scheme
**Axioms.** We retain four axioms on reward-system function [@schultz2016dopamine; @tobler2005adaptive]:

- **A1 Ancestral Calibration.** In the ancestral signal distribution $S_\mathrm{anc}$, proximate reward $U_\mathrm{perc} = \varphi(s, \mathbf{g})$ is a monotone noisy encoder of fitness-relevant value $U_\mathrm{fit}$. Encoding is receptor-mediated and specified by genetically inherited parameters $\mathbf{g}$.
- **A2 Environmental Decoupling.** After separation time $t_\mathrm{sep}$, novel signals in $S_\mathrm{mod}$ fall outside $S_\mathrm{anc}$, the correlation $\rho(U_\mathrm{perc}, U_\mathrm{fit})$ drops below a critical threshold, and the wedge $\Delta_{ST}$ emerges.
- **A3 Endorsement Inertia.** Behaviour $b = \arg\max [(1 - w) U_\mathrm{perc} + w \, \mathbb{E}[U_\mathrm{fit} \mid B]]$ with $w \le w_\mathrm{max} < 1/2$. In the animal limit $w \to 0$, behaviour is driven by proximate reward.
- **A4 Partial Cost Visibility.** Effective cost at horizon $\tau$ is $c_\mathrm{eff}(\tau) = \delta(\tau) c$ with $\delta(0) = 1$ and $\delta' < 0$; for $\tau \gg T_\mathrm{gen}$, $\delta(\tau) \to 0$.

**Operational scalar.** $\Delta_{ST,i} \equiv U_{\mathrm{perc},i}(s^*) - \mathbb{E}[U_{\mathrm{fit},i}(s^*)]$ is evaluated at the modal population behaviour $s^*$ in the species' contemporary environment. The scalar is signed, continuous, and cross-species comparable.

**F1–F4 coding scheme.** A case enters the corpus if it satisfies all four signatures: F1 reward signal detectable; F2 voluntary approach / endorsement; F3 fitness cost; F4 endorsement maintained under cost.

**H_universal_1 prediction (conditional on universality).** Under the decomposition $\Delta_{ST,i} = \gamma g_i + \varepsilon_i(S_{\mathrm{mod},i})$, BM-plus-error modelling yields $\mathbb{E}[K] = \gamma^2 \sigma_g^2 / (\gamma^2 \sigma_g^2 + \sigma_\varepsilon^2 / \bar{\sigma}_\Sigma)$. Bounding $\sigma_\varepsilon^2 / (\gamma^2 \sigma_g^2) \in [0.4, 2.5]$ from A1 and A2 yields $K \in [0.29, 0.71]$ with point prediction $K \approx 0.5$. Under uniform, log-uniform, and Jeffreys priors, the axioms imply that, *if Sweet Trap is a universal Metazoan trait*, $K$ should exceed 0.30 with prior probability $\ge 0.92$. Full derivation in Supplementary Note S1.

## 2.2 Existence layer — human Sweet Trap via trans-ancestry Mendelian randomization
The human MR analyses test the existence of reward-fitness decoupling at the *Homo sapiens* tip, not its universality across Metazoa. **Cohorts and ancestry coverage.** Primary causal estimates use European-ancestry mortality GWAS [@Pilling2017]; non-European confirmation uses East Asian (ukb-e-4501_EAS, $n = 1{,}122$) and African-ancestry (ukb-e-1807_AFR, $n = 3{,}732$) UK Biobank sub-cohorts. Exposure GWAS draw from Yengo et al. 2018 BMI (ieu-b-40), Liu et al. 2019 GSCAN alcohol-drinks-per-week (ieu-b-73), UK Biobank leisure screen-time (ukb-b-5192) and sugar intake (ukb-b-5237). **Exposure-outcome pairs.** Four pre-registered chains: BMI $\to$ all-cause mortality; sugar $\to$ cardiovascular mortality; leisure screen-time $\to$ all-cause mortality; alcohol $\to$ all-cause mortality. **MR methods.** Primary inference is inverse-variance-weighted random-effects MR, triangulated against MR-Egger [@bowden2015mregger], weighted-median [@bowden2016weightedmedian], simple mode, and weighted mode. Steiger filtering, MR-Egger intercept, Cochran's Q, leave-one-out, and Radial-MR are reported. **Trans-ancestry meta-analysis.** Per-chain log-ORs are pooled via DerSimonian-Laird random-effects (`metafor::rma`). **Falsification criterion.** Existence at the human tip fails if $\le 2$ of 4 chains show concordant direction or if pooled OR 95% CI crosses 1 on $\ge 2$ of 4 chains.

## 2.3 H_universal_1 — animal phylogenetic signal
**Systematic search.** PRISMA-ScR protocol on PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, and bioRxiv (1995–2026). Inclusion: F1–F4 satisfied with primary-study or directly-cited evidence. Cases coded on phylum, class, order, species; mechanism route (A ancestral-calibration failure, B supernormal, C sensory exploitation); evidence-quality tier; $\Delta_{ST}$ magnitude on a 0–1 interval. Two author-coders code all cases; three external coders re-code a blinded 30% subset (Fleiss' $\kappa$ target $\ge 0.70$). Target $N \ge 50$ cases spanning $\ge 6$ phyla.

**Phylogeny.** Species-level time-calibrated phylogeny extracted from TimeTree 5 [@kumar2022timetree] with cross-validation against Open Tree of Life [@hinchliff2015synthesis]. Taxonomic harmonisation via GBIF backbone.

**Phylogenetic signal.** Blomberg's $K$ [@blomberg2003testing] and Pagel's $\lambda$ [@pagel1999inferring] computed via `phytools::phylosig` with 9,999-replicate permutation tests. Likelihood-ratio test of $\lambda = 0$ versus MLE $\lambda$. Moran's $I$ as additional spatial-autocorrelation diagnostic. Ancestral-state reconstruction via stochastic character mapping (SIMMAP) [@bollback2006simmap]. Subset analyses on Chordata-only, Chordata+Arthropoda, and Arthropoda-only species sets.

**Falsification criterion (universality).** H_universal_1 fails if either (a) cross-phylum $K \le 0.30$ or (b) $\lambda$ 95% CI includes 0. The pre-specified strong-falsification floor is $K < 0.10$ AND $\lambda$ 95% CI includes 0.

## 2.4 H_universal_2 — molecular substrate sharing across phyla
**Target families.** Sweet-taste receptors only in this paper: vertebrate *TAS1R1*, *TAS1R2*, *TAS1R3* (Chordata) and insect *Gr5a*–*Gr64* family (Arthropoda). Within-phylum dopamine-receptor (DRD/DopR) panel reported as descriptive baseline (Chordata, Arthropoda, Mollusca, Cnidaria).

**Ortholog recovery.** OrthoFinder2 [@emms2019orthofinder] on Ensembl, Ensembl Metazoa, and OrthoDB [@kuznetsov2023orthodb], validated against NCBI HomoloGene and Ensembl Compara.

**H_universal_2 primary test.** Jaccard similarity of InterPro Pfam-domain sets [@blum2021interpro] between vertebrate TAS1R and insect Gr sweet-receptor families. *If reward-receptor architecture is universal across phyla, Jaccard similarity should exceed the matched-random Pfam null baseline.* The null baseline is generated by sampling 10,000 random pairs of one-to-one orthologs of similar length and expression breadth from the same two genomes; the empirical Jaccard distribution is computed over this null and compared to the observed reward-family Jaccard. Pre-registered threshold: observed Jaccard $\ge 0.50$ AND $> 99$th percentile of the null.

**Within-phylum descriptive baseline.** Pfam-architecture consistency within DRD (Chordata) and DopR (Arthropoda) is reported descriptively. We do not interpret within-phylum architectural fixation as universality evidence; aminergic Class-A GPCR architecture is known to predate the cnidarian/bilaterian split [@kamesh2008gpcr] and its consistency across descendants is expected under either inheritance or universal architecture.

**Falsification criterion (universality).** H_universal_2 fails if observed Jaccard $\le$ null 50th percentile. Strong-falsification floor: observed Jaccard $\le 0.05$.

## 2.5 H_universal_3 — cross-clade positive selection
**Target genes.** Sweet-taste receptors (*TAS1R1*, *TAS1R3*, *Gr_sweet*); the dopamine, opioid, and orexin panels did not pass the codon-alignment readiness gate at submission and are deferred to Paper 2 (Deviation #4).

**Branch-site test.** PAML codeml branch-site Model A versus null (Model A with $\omega_2 = 1$) [@yang2007paml; @zhang2005branchsite; @anisimova2007branchsite] on 6 ecological-shift production clades: *Apis mellifera* nectarivore Gr_sweet; *Drosophila melanogaster* Gr64; *Drosophila* all-Grs; Lepidoptera; Coleoptera; *Aedes aegypti*. Pre-registered Bonferroni threshold: $\alpha = 0.05 / (15 \times 4) = 0.00083$. *If reward-receptor selection is universal across ecological-shift lineages, multiple production clades should show LRT exceeding the Bonferroni threshold.* A randomised-starting-$\omega$ diagnostic [@anisimova2007branchsite] is run on every clade returning LRT = 0 to distinguish biological null from optimiser convergence at the boundary. We classify a foreground $\omega_{2a} > 30$ on a clade with $n \le 7$ taxa as falling within the **small-clade boundary-artefact range** [@anisimova2007branchsite], based on their reported instability ranges for foreground $\omega$ estimates on small alignments; this operational threshold is applied throughout §3.4 and §4.5 to flag results requiring expanded-taxon replication.

**Positive control.** Hummingbird *TAS1R1* must be re-detected with $\omega > 1$ and LRT $p < 0.01$ [@baldwin2014hummingbird] for downstream inference to be valid.

**Falsification criterion (universality).** H_universal_3 fails if 0 or 1 of 6 production clades clear the Bonferroni threshold. Single-clade signal is recorded as *lineage-specific*, not universal.

# 3 Results
## 3.1 Existence is widespread (Layer 1: SUPPORTED)
**Layer 1 (existence): SUPPORTED.** Sweet Trap is widely distributed across Metazoa. Our PRISMA-ScR search identified 260 records; 252 survived deduplication, 216 were assessed at full text, 23 were pdf-unavailable (Deviation row #5) and 20 failed F1–F4, yielding **114 F1+F2-passing cases** — double the pre-registered $\ge 50$ target — across **7 phyla**: Chordata ($n = 62$), Arthropoda ($n = 35$), Nematoda (5), Cnidaria (5), Mollusca (4), Annelida (2), multi-phylum (1) (Figure @fig:2 panel a; archive `03-analysis/part2-prisma/outputs/animal_cases_final.csv`). Two-coder inter-rater reliability for F1–F4 was computed on the full corpus; blinded external three-coder recoding on a 30% subset is deferred (§4.6 Limitations). Mapping each case to a TimeTree 5 tip and averaging $\Delta_{ST}$ within-species yielded an analytic sample of $n = 56$ species (71 contributing cases). Reward-fitness decoupling, on this evidence base, is *not rare*: it appears in mammals, birds, reptiles, fishes, insects, nematodes, cnidarians, molluscs, and annelids. The existence-layer prediction is met with substantial margin.

## 3.2 Cross-phylum phylogenetic signal is not detected (Layer 2: REFUTED)
**Our pre-registered Layer 2 prediction ($K > 0.30$) is refuted.** Three independent statistics on the 56-species cross-phylum tree return null: Blomberg's $K = 0.117$ (95% bootstrap CI [0.056, 0.169]; $p = 0.251$; 9,999 permutations); Pagel's $\lambda = 0.0001$ (95% CI [0.0001, 0.413]; LRT $p = 1.00$); Moran's $I = -0.032$ ($p = 0.626$). The pre-registered universality prediction ($P(K > 0.30) \ge 0.92$, point expectation $K \approx 0.5$, range [0.29, 0.71]) is not met. The observation lies above the strong-falsification floor ($K < 0.10$) by 0.017 but is well below the predicted effect zone, and $\lambda$'s 95% CI does include zero. We confront this result rather than reframe it: BM-style phylogenetic inheritance of $\Delta_{ST}$ at the $K > 0.30$ level is *not supported* by these data. The universality-as-inheritance reading of Sweet Trap is refuted.

**Subgroup robustness.** The cross-phylum null replicates in vertebrate-dominated subsets — Chordata only ($n = 37$, $K = 0.122$, $p = 0.51$); Chordata + Arthropoda ($n = 50$, $K = 0.105$, $p = 0.41$); $\lambda \approx 0$ in both — indicating the null is not an artefact of phylum-level sampling imbalance.

**Within-Arthropoda boundary case.** Within Arthropoda alone ($n = 13$ species), Blomberg's $K = 1.446$ ($p = 0.007$) and Pagel's $\lambda = 1.162$ ($p = 0.020$), driven by elevated $\Delta_{ST}$ in phototactic insect orders (Lepidoptera, Coleoptera, Hemiptera) sharing artificial-light-at-night vulnerability. We report this as a *boundary case*, not as universality evidence: the pipeline can detect signal where ecological exposure clusters by clade, which confirms that the cross-phylum null is not attributable to a dysfunctional test, but it does not rescue universality across Metazoa. The within-Arthropoda signal is mechanistically informative for Paper 2 — it identifies one ecological-niche signature (shared phototactic susceptibility) under which lineage-specific Sweet Trap can phylogenetically cluster — but does not by itself argue that Sweet Trap is a Metazoan-wide inherited trait.

## 3.3 Cross-phylum molecular substrate is not shared (Layer 3: NOT SUPPORTED)
**Layer 3 (shared substrate): NOT SUPPORTED.** Vertebrate TAS1R sweet receptors and insect Gr sweet receptors carry zero shared Pfam domains. The Jaccard similarity between their Pfam-domain sets is **0.00** on 13 chordate TAS1R sequences (4 species) and 38 insect Gr sequences (6 species spanning Diptera, Coleoptera, Lepidoptera, Hymenoptera). Vertebrate TAS1Rs carry the Class-C 7TM / ANF Venus-flytrap / NCD3G triad {PF00003, PF01094, PF07562}; insect Grs carry the insect-specific 7TM_6 domain {PF06151}. The two families share no Pfam domain.

**Against the matched-random Pfam null baseline, Jaccard = 0 is the modal expected outcome under our pre-registered null baseline, not an enrichment.** Sampling 10,000 random pairs of one-to-one orthologs of similar length and expression breadth from the same two reference genomes returns Jaccard = 0 with empirical probability $P(\text{Jaccard} = 0 \mid \text{null}) = 0.9998$. The observed reward-family Jaccard is therefore *what null predicts*; it does not exceed the null at any threshold. The pre-registered universality prediction (observed Jaccard $> 99$th percentile of null AND $\ge 0.50$) is not met. Non-shared molecular substrate is the most parsimonious descriptive reading: vertebrate TAS1R and insect Gr arrived at sweet detection on non-orthologous molecular parts, and the data do not support a shared cross-phylum architecture. We note that this is not the same as saying TAS1R and Gr are unrelated phenomena — both detect sugars and trigger appetitive behaviour — but the molecular machinery that does so is not shared between the two phyla. Universality at the receptor-substrate layer is refuted.

**Within-phylum architectural conservation (descriptive baseline).** The within-phylum receptor panel is reported as a descriptive pattern, not as convergence or universality evidence. Of 35 candidate dopamine-receptor sequences across 4 phyla, 25 (3 phyla — Chordata: 10; Arthropoda: 12; Mollusca: 3) qualify as true DRD orthologs after OrthoFinder + phylogenetic-classification filtering. All 25 carry Pfam PF00001. Extending to 14 non-DRD aminergic receptors (octopamine/serotonin/$\beta$-adrenergic; mollusc and cnidarian), all 39 candidate aminergic Class-A GPCRs carry PF00001 architecture. This is consistent with the well-known result that aminergic Class-A GPCR architecture predates the cnidarian/bilaterian split [@kamesh2008gpcr; @Anctil2009; @hayakawa2022mbe]. We do not interpret PF00001 fixation as convergence evidence — it is the expected outcome under deep-time stabilising selection on a single ancestral gene family — and we do not interpret it as universality evidence for Sweet Trap, because aminergic GPCR architecture is shared across animals regardless of whether those animals show reward-fitness decoupling. This is a within-phylum descriptive baseline, not a universality signal (Table @tbl:arch).

## 3.4 Lineage-specific positive selection signal — optimiser-sensitive (Layer 4: INCONCLUSIVE)
**Of 6 production clades tested, no clade yields robust positive-selection signal after optimiser-boundary diagnostic interrogation. 3/6 are robustly null and 3/6 are optimiser-sensitive (boundary-escape behaviour at one or more $\omega_0$ starts, with foreground $\omega$ in the small-clade boundary-artefact range, $\omega > 30$ on $n \le 7$ taxa). The pre-registered universality prediction (multiple clades) is not supported, but the failure mode is insufficient method power, not confirmed absence of selection.** We ran 21 PAML branch-site tests on sweet-taste receptors across the 6 production clades. The *Apis mellifera* nectarivore Gr_sweet clade returned LRT = 9.92 (df = 1; $p_\mathrm{raw} = 8.2 \times 10^{-4}$; Bonferroni-prereg $p = 0.049$, realised-$\alpha$ Bonferroni $p = 0.017$, BH $q = 0.006$; foreground $\omega_{2a} = 36.2$ with 12.1% of sites in the positive-selection class; 2 BEB sites at $P \ge 0.90$ and zero at $P \ge 0.95$). Five remaining clade foregrounds — *Drosophila* Gr64, *Drosophila* all-Grs, Lepidoptera, Coleoptera, *Aedes* — returned LRT = 0.000 with foreground $\omega$ collapsing to the null-model boundary at $\omega_2 = 1.0$.

**Optimiser-boundary diagnostic on the LRT = 0 clades.** We interrogated the LRT = 0 outcomes for optimiser-boundary sensitivity using a randomised starting-$\omega_0 \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0\}$ diagnostic per @anisimova2007branchsite (5 clades $\times$ 5 starts = 25 codeml runs; 17.5 wall-hours). Of 5 production clades returning LRT = 0, **3 are robustly null** (*Coleoptera*, *Lepidoptera*, *Aedes aegypti*: identical lnL across all 5 starts, foreground $\omega_{2a} = 1.0$ throughout). **2 are optimiser-sensitive** (*Drosophila* Gr64 cluster: lnL escape at $\omega_0 = 5.0$ with LRT = 9.68 and $\omega_{2a} = 144.9$; *Drosophila* all-Grs: escape at $\omega_0 = 5.0$ with LRT = 4.33 and $\omega_{2a} = 95.1$). Both *Drosophila* foreground $\omega_{2a}$ values lie within the small-clade boundary-artefact range ($\omega > 30$ on $n \le 7$ taxa, §2.5) [@anisimova2007branchsite]. We treat the *Drosophila* escapes as **optimiser-sensitive results**, not as confirmed positive-selection signals — the only way to distinguish would be expanded-taxon BUSTED / aBSREL / RELAX triangulation, committed for Paper 2.

**Optimiser-boundary diagnostic on the *Apis* production-positive clade (asymmetric rule).** We then applied the same starting-$\omega_0$ diagnostic to the *Apis mellifera* Gr_sweet clade — the only LRT > 0 production result, with $\omega = 36.2$ sitting in the small-clade boundary-artefact range. Of 5 $\omega_0$ starts, **4 converge to the production basin** ($\ln L_\mathrm{alt} = -52{,}163.16$, LRT = 9.92, $\omega_{2a} = 36.18$) — confirming the production result is reproducible across most starts. **However, the $\omega_0 = 5.0$ start escapes to a sub-null basin** ($\ln L_\mathrm{alt} = -52{,}169.36$, LRT = $-2.48$, $\omega_{2a}$ collapsing to 1.63 with the positive-selection site class vanishing entirely). The *Apis* signal therefore joins the two *Drosophila* clades as **optimiser-sensitive**, leaving **0 of 6 production clades with robust positive-selection signal** (3 robustly null; 3 optimiser-sensitive). No clade qualifies as robust positive selection, and the headline pre-registered universality prediction (multiple production clades exceeding Bonferroni) is not supported.

**Pipeline validation.** The same pipeline reproduces the @baldwin2014hummingbird hummingbird TAS1R1 positive control at LRT = 55.9 ($p < 4 \times 10^{-14}$) on the Apodiformes clade and at LRT = 61.06 on the hummingbird-only clade; the *Mus musculus* negative control returns LRT = 0.0. Positive selection on this reduced repertoire would be a signal on a biologically-distinct, lineage-specific set of receptors associated with obligate nectarivory, paralleling but not orthologous to the hummingbird TAS1R1 case. We make no such claim from Paper-1 evidence: with the production result now classified APIS_OPTIMIZER_ARTIFACT, *Apis* is one of three optimiser-sensitive escapes pending mechanistic disambiguation in Paper 2, not a lineage-specific exemplar of confirmed positive selection.

## 3.5 Synthesis: widespread but not universal
Three of four pre-registered layers test universality and three of three are not supported. Existence (Layer 1) is supported with substantial margin. The pattern is not "Sweet Trap is a Metazoan synapomorphy"; it is "Sweet Trap is widespread but not universal: it occurs across many lineages but does not behave as inherited or shared at the molecular level." **INCONCLUSIVE — optimiser-sensitive.** After randomised-starting-$\omega$ diagnostic on all 6 production clades [@anisimova2007branchsite], the Layer-4 matrix resolves as: 3/6 robustly null (*Coleoptera*, *Lepidoptera*, *Aedes*), and **3/6 optimiser-sensitive** (*Apis* Gr_sweet, *Drosophila* Gr64 cluster, *Drosophila* all-Grs — all show boundary-escape behaviour at one or more $\omega_0$ starts, with foreground $\omega_{2a}$ in the small-clade boundary-artefact range, $\omega > 30$ on $n \le 7$ taxa). **No clade qualifies as robust positive selection.** The pre-registered universality prediction ($\ge 2$ robust positives across ecological-shift clades) is not supported, but the failure mode is **insufficient method power on this alignment**, not confirmed absence of selection. The synthesis verdict — Sweet Trap is widespread but not universal — is unaffected because it rests on Layer 2 (cross-phylum $K = 0.117$) and Layer 3 (Pfam null-baseline non-enrichment), not on Layer 4 alone.

Read together, these results favour a **lineage-specific origins** model: reward-fitness decoupling re-emerges wherever local ecological conditions decouple proximate signals from realized fitness, and the molecular machinery on which it rides is whichever reward-receptor family the lineage has available. Two internal data points are individually consistent with this reading without by themselves confirming it: (i) within-Arthropoda $K = 1.446$ shows phylogenetic signal *can* be detected where ecological exposure (ALAN phototaxis) clusters by clade — i.e., signal is a function of niche, not of ancestry — and (ii) the *Apis* Gr_sweet signal is most parsimoniously read as selection specific to a Hymenopteran nectarivore lineage, paralleling but not orthologous to the hummingbird TAS1R1 case. Paper 2 will test the lineage-specific reading on an independent corpus by characterising the ecological-niche conditions (HIREC severity, generation time, gene-family pre-conditions) under which Sweet Trap emerges, and will commit to the predictions before that data is collected.

# 4 Discussion

## 4.1 Headline: widespread but not universal
All three universality predictions are inconclusive or refuted: cross-phylum phylogenetic signal refuted ($K = 0.117$, $p = 0.251$); cross-phylum molecular substrate non-enriched against null (Jaccard = 0; $P(=0 \mid \text{null}) = 0.9998$); and lineage-specific positive selection inconclusive — **no clade among 6 production tests yields robust positive-selection signal after optimiser-boundary diagnostic** (3/6 robustly null; 3/6 optimiser-sensitive; 0/6 robust positives; all three non-zero signals lie in the small-clade boundary-artefact range, $\omega > 30$ on $n \le 7$ taxa). The same dataset confirms widespread *existence*: 114 cases / 7 phyla / 56 species satisfy F1–F4, and four trans-ancestry MR chains confirm the phenomenon at the *Homo sapiens* tip.

The integration is not "Sweet Trap is convergent across Metazoa." It is "Sweet Trap is a real, distributed, lineage-specific phenomenon." We treat this as the cleanest available empirical answer to the meta-question raised in §1: reward-fitness decoupling is not a universal feature of animal reward systems. It is a recurring local outcome whose emergence is governed by ecological-niche conditions rather than by phylogenetic descent. The three readings of §1 — inherited, conserved, convergent-as-shared-trait — are all weakened by this evidence; the fourth reading, *lineage-specific origins under shared selective constraint*, is the most parsimonious surviving alternative, and it is exactly the reading that Paper 2 is designed to test on independent data.

## 4.2 Why is the cross-phylum prediction refuted? Three readings, lineage-specific origins is most parsimonious
**Our pre-registered phylogenetic-signal prediction failed.** The pre-registered A1–A4-derived expectation was $P(K > 0.30) \ge 0.92$ with point value $K \approx 0.5$ (Supplementary Note S1); the observed cross-phylum $K = 0.117$ ($p = 0.251$; $\lambda \approx 0$; $n = 56$ species) is not in that zone. Honestly reported, our axioms generated a prediction that the data did not vindicate at the cross-phylum scale. Three interpretations are open, presented in order of increasing parsimony rather than favourability.

The **lineage-specific origins** reading is the most parsimonious. If $\Delta_{ST}$ tracks the mismatch between each lineage's *current* reward architecture and its *current* signal environment, rather than tracking ancestry, then cross-phylum $K \to 0$ is the predicted pattern. The theoretical literature on convergence under shared selective constraint anticipates this signature precisely [@stern2013genetic; @losos2011convergence; @speedarbuckle2017convergence]: parallel emergence of a similar functional outcome in lineages exposed to similar selective conditions, on whatever molecular machinery happens to be available locally. We frame these citations as supporting the existence of *parallel emergence under shared selective constraint*, not as supporting our own data as direct convergence evidence — that interpretation is generated by the failure of the universal predictions, not registered in advance, and must be tested against an independent corpus in Paper 2 (§4.6 Limitations).

The **Type II error** reading remains live but limited. Blomberg's $K$ at $n \approx 56$ species has reduced power to detect signal at true $\lambda \in (0.3, 0.7)$ [@munkemuller2012brief]. However, three internal robustness features make the Type II reading unlikely to be the whole story: (a) the within-Arthropoda subset shows that the test does detect strong signal where ecological exposure clusters by clade ($K = 1.446$, $p = 0.007$, $n = 13$) — i.e., the test is not failing due to sample-size alone; (b) the cross-phylum null replicates in two further sub-samples (Chordata only, Chordata+Arthropoda); and (c) the molecular and selection results (Layers 3 and 4) are also not supported — three independent layers do not accidentally fail in the same direction under Type II error. The Type II reading might rescue Layer 2 in isolation but it does not rescue the universal version of Sweet Trap when the failure is replicated across all three universality layers.

The **BM-inheritance reading is wrong** is the most aggressive but cannot be excluded. The A1–A4-derived prior on $\gamma^2 \sigma_g^2 / (\gamma^2 \sigma_g^2 + \sigma_\varepsilon^2 / \bar{\sigma}_\Sigma)$ may have over-weighted ancestry relative to environmental variance, in which case the predictive model itself is mis-specified. We note this honestly: a falsified prediction can also be a sign that the framework that generated it should be reconsidered, and Paper 2 will treat the lineage-specific origins reading as a positive prediction to be tested directly rather than as a residual interpretation of a failed universality test.

## 4.3 Within-phylum architectural conservation is a descriptive baseline, not convergence evidence
Aminergic Class-A GPCR architecture is shared across Bilateria. Pfam PF00001 is retained at 100% across 25 tree-confirmed bilaterian DRD orthologs in 3 phyla (Chordata, Arthropoda, Mollusca) [@yamamoto2011evolution]. Extending to 14 non-DRD aminergic candidates (octopamine, serotonin, $\beta$-adrenergic) all carrying PF00001 pushes architectural conservation back to the cnidarian-bilaterian last common ancestor (~700 Myr; 39 sequences; 4 phyla) [@kamesh2008gpcr; @Anctil2009]. We report this pattern as a *descriptive baseline* rather than as evidence for any universality reading of Sweet Trap. Conservation at this depth is consistent with deep-time stabilising selection on a single ancestral gene family that radiated before the split of any lineage we now observe; it does not by itself argue that the resulting receptors carry universal reward-fitness decoupling architecture, because aminergic GPCR architecture is shared across animals regardless of whether any given animal shows reward-fitness decoupling. Within-phylum architectural fixation tells us about the stability of an ancient receptor family, not about universality of an emergent behavioural phenomenon.

## 4.4 Cross-phylum molecular substrate is non-shared, consistent with parallel functional invention from non-orthologous parts
Vertebrate TAS1Rs and insect Grs are paraphyletic at the molecular level: vertebrate TAS1Rs assemble Class-C GPCR heterodimers (PF00003 + PF01094 + PF07562) while insect Grs use 7TM_6 (PF06151), and the cross-phylum Pfam Jaccard is zero [@fredriksson2003repertoire; @robertson2003fly]. The two families share no Pfam domain. A sweet-taste detection phenotype has emerged in both vertebrate TAS1R and insect Gr lineages, on non-orthologous molecular parts, in lineages separated by >550 Myr. We are nonetheless careful in how we read this. The matched-random Pfam null baseline (§2.4) shows that disjointness ($P(\text{Jaccard} = 0 \mid \text{null}) = 0.9998$) is the modal expectation under our pre-registered null baseline for any pair of randomly-selected one-to-one orthologs from these two genomes: the observed Jaccard = 0 is what the null predicts and is therefore not statistical convergence evidence. The data are *consistent with* parallel functional emergence from non-orthologous parts but cannot statistically establish convergence given the matched-random null baseline; the substrate-level pattern is non-shared rather than shared, and cannot be made into a universality claim. Within Stern's framework [@stern2013genetic], lineage-specific origins on non-orthologous molecular parts is one admissible reading; within Speed and Arbuckle's framework [@speedarbuckle2017convergence] it is the weakest available form of architectural similarity. We do not claim Paper-1 evidence supports a convergence reading at the strong-statistical level; we claim the data refute the universality reading, and the lineage-specific origins reading — independent functional inventions on non-orthologous parts — is one alternative reading consistent with the observed pattern.

## 4.5 No robust positive selection across 6 tests — branch-site Model A is power-limited on this alignment
Following the optimiser-boundary diagnostic on all 6 production clades, we find **no robust positive-selection signal** distinguishable from optimiser sensitivity across any tested clade. The *Apis* Gr_sweet result — long flagged as the most plausible candidate — replicates across 4 of 5 $\omega_0$ starts at LRT = 9.92, $\omega = 36.18$, but a single start ($\omega_0 = 5.0$) escapes to a sub-null basin where the alt model fits worse than the null and the positive-selection site class vanishes. Three of six production clades are now in this optimiser-sensitive category (*Apis* Gr_sweet, *D. melanogaster* Gr64 cluster $\omega = 144.9$, *D. melanogaster* all-Grs $\omega = 95.1$), all with foreground $\omega$ in the small-clade boundary-artefact range ($\omega > 30$ on $n \le 7$ taxa, §2.5). The remaining three clades (*Coleoptera*, *Lepidoptera*, *Aedes*) are robustly null across all five starts.

**The honest reading is that branch-site Model A on this alignment cannot resolve the universality question** — it produces no signal robust enough to either confirm or refute lineage-specific positive selection. Our pipeline integrity is independently verified: the @baldwin2014hummingbird hummingbird TAS1R1 positive control reproduces at LRT = 55.9 on Apodiformes ($p < 4 \times 10^{-14}$), and the *Mus musculus* negative control returns LRT = 0. The failure mode is not pipeline error but the interaction of branch-site Model A with small foreground clades ($n = 4$ to 7 taxa) on a 38-taxon × 3,234-codon alignment, where the likelihood surface admits multiple optima and the positive-selection class is recovered only intermittently across $\omega_0$ starts.

The mechanistic prediction we initially attached to *Apis* (extended *Apis*–*Bombus*–*Vespa* replication on $n \ge 10$ with BUSTED / aBSREL / RELAX triangulation [@murrell2015busted; @smith2015absrel; @wertheim2015relax]) now applies symmetrically to all three optimiser-sensitive clades, all of which become Paper 2 priorities. If *Apis* replication holds under triangulation, three structural predictions follow for Paper 3 (altered ligand-binding affinities; nectar-concentration response breadth; concentration of derived amino-acid states on *Apis* terminal branches), with codons 533 and 768 as the first structural entry points; if it fails, the lineage-specific reading loses its most plausible mechanistic exemplar but the universality verdict is unchanged. Whether each of the three optimiser-sensitive clades harbours genuine positive selection invisible to branch-site Model A on this alignment, or genuinely null evolution, remains the open question.

**Disclosure on H6a vs the pre-registered threshold.** The OSF pre-registration (`gantt_12_weeks.md` Gate-1 row, deposited 2026-04-24 in `sweet_trap_v4_stage6_preregistration.zip`) specified the H6a threshold as "$\ge 1$ positive-selected gene on $\ge 1$ lineage at branch-site Bonferroni-corrected significance". Strictly read against this threshold, the *Apis* Gr_sweet production result (LRT = 9.92, $p_\text{Bonf-prereg} = 0.049$, $\omega = 36.2$ on $n = 4$ taxa) satisfies the pre-registered H6a Gate. We nevertheless report Layer 4 as INCONCLUSIVE rather than as a strict pre-registered pass, because the post-hoc optimiser-boundary diagnostic (§3.4; FigS1) revealed the *Apis* result is not robust to $\omega_0$ start choice and 5/6 production clades fail the diagnostic. This downgrade is **more conservative** than our pre-registration required, not less; we elected to report it because the diagnostic-revealed instability is, in our judgement, a more truthful representation of what the data support than the strict pre-registered pass would be. Readers preferring the strict pre-registered reading should note that under that reading, H6a is partially supported (1/6 clades pass at Bonferroni-prereg $p < 0.05$); under our diagnostic-augmented reading, no clade qualifies as robust positive selection.

## 4.6 Limitations
*Lineage-specific origins reading was not pre-registered as an alternative.* The lineage-specific origins reframing in §4.2 and §4.5 is *generated* by the failure of the universal predictions; it was not pre-registered as an alternative prediction that the universality nulls could confirm. Per Popperian best practice [@kerr1998harking; @nosek2018preregistration; @munafo2017manifesto], we therefore classify the lineage-specific reading as **post-hoc and hypothesis-generating**, and Paper 2 will commit to its quantitative predictions before the independent corpus is collected. *H_universal_2 narrowed scope.* We tested cross-phylum substrate sharing only on TAS1R/Gr sweet-receptor families; photoreceptor, olfactory, opioid, and cognitive-reward families implicated in the §1 flagship cases (moths, turtles, UPF, digital feeds) are deferred to Paper 2. The universality verdict on H_universal_2 is therefore strictly bounded to sweet-taste detection; we have not refuted shared substrate across all reward modalities, only across the one we tested. *H_universal_3 no robust positives.* No production clade among the six tested yields robust positive-selection signal after the optimiser-boundary diagnostic; the *Apis* result reproduces at 4/5 starts but escapes to a sub-null basin at $\omega_0 = 5.0$ (APIS_OPTIMIZER_ARTIFACT). All three optimiser-sensitive clades (Apis Gr_sweet, dmel Gr64 cluster, dmel all-Grs) require expanded-taxon BUSTED / aBSREL / RELAX triangulation in Paper 2 to disambiguate genuine positive selection from optimiser sensitivity. *H_universal_1 power.* Blomberg's $K$ at $n \approx 56$ species has reduced power to detect $\lambda \in (0.3, 0.7)$ [@munkemuller2012brief]; we mitigate with two subset replications (Chordata only, Chordata+Arthropoda) and the within-Arthropoda detection of strong signal where it exists. *Existence-layer ancestry coverage.* Primary causal estimates use EUR GWAS with EAS ($n = 1{,}122$) and AFR ($n = 3{,}732$) UKB sub-cohorts; SAS and AMR mortality GWAS unavailable as of 2026-04-24; nominal between-ancestry $I^2 = 0$ is non-informative given precision (§3.6 footnote). *UPF instrumentation.* A fifth pre-registered pair (UPF $\to$ mortality) via processed-meat proxy returned OR = 0.906 ($p = 0.064$), attributable to healthy-eater bias in diet-quality GWAS [@Monteiro2018_NOVA]; deferred pending NOVA-4 PGS. *Inter-rater reliability.* Two author-coders screened all 114 cases against pre-registered F1–F4; blinded external three-coder recoding on a 30% subset is not delivered in Paper 1 and is committed as a Paper-1 OSF addendum within 30 days of RSOS final publication. *PRISMA corpus.* 23 of 216 candidates (10.6%) excluded as pdf-unavailable; Cnidaria ($n = 5$), Mollusca ($n = 4$) and Annelida ($n = 2$) coverage is thin. *Cnidarian DRD gap.* Whole-proteome reciprocal BLASTP across 100,892 cnidarian proteins recovered no true DRD orthologs [@Anctil2009; @hayakawa2022mbe]; the canonical DRD radiation is bilaterian-specific. *Branch-site test power on large alignments with small foreground clades.* Our optimiser-boundary diagnostic [@anisimova2007branchsite, 30 codeml runs over ~19 wall-hours: 25 on the 5 LRT = 0 clades + 5 on the *Apis* production-positive clade] revealed boundary-escape behaviour in **3 of 6 production clades**: both *Drosophila* clades show LRT > 0 escapes only at $\omega_0 = 5.0$ ($\omega_{2a} = 144.9$ and 95.1) and *Apis* shows a near-null escape only at $\omega_0 = 5.0$ (LRT = $-2.48$). All three escape values lie in the small-clade boundary-artefact range ($\omega > 30$ on $n \le 7$ taxa). This indicates that branch-site Model A on the 38-taxon × 3,234-codon Gr_sweet alignment with 4–7-taxon foregrounds is **dominated by optimiser behaviour at the boundary**, not by clean biological signal. The Layer-4 universality test is therefore inconclusive between true biological null (no selection on these clades) and insufficient method power (selection present but undetectable on this alignment). BUSTED / aBSREL / RELAX triangulation in Paper 2 is the principal route to disambiguation.

## 4.7 Future directions
Paper 2's primary mission is to identify the **lineage-specific conditions** under which Sweet Trap emerges and to test those conditions on an independent case corpus. Pre-registered candidate triggers include severity of ecological niche shift (obligate vs facultative reward-based niche); presence of human-induced rapid environmental change (HIREC) pressure [@robertson2013ecological; @sih2011evolution]; generation time relative to environmental-change rate; gene-family architectural pre-conditions (whether the lineage carries a reward-receptor family with the structural flexibility to remodel under selection). H_universal_1 will be re-tested on a fresh case corpus with PGLS-adjusted covariates (ALAN exposure, body mass, generation time, publication period); H_universal_2 will be extended to olfactory, opioid, photoreceptor and cognitive-reward receptor families implicated in §1 flagship cases (moths, turtles, UPF, digital feeds); H_universal_3 will be replicated by BUSTED / aBSREL / RELAX triangulation on three boundary-range escapes — the *Apis* sweet-receptor clade with extended *Apis*–*Bombus*–*Vespa* $n \ge 10$ taxon set, plus the two *Drosophila* clades (Gr64 cluster, all-Grs) flagged as optimiser-sensitive in our diagnostic — to disambiguate genuine positive selection from boundary artefacts. *Paper 3* commits to direct receptor-knockdown behavioural-rescue experiments in *Nematostella*, *Aplysia*, and bumblebee systems — the mechanistic evidence reserved from Paper 1.

## 4.8 Closing
We pre-registered three predictions of cross-metazoan universality for reward-fitness decoupling and tested all three with a single integrated dataset. All three are not supported. Existence is supported with substantial margin: 114 cases across 7 phyla and 56 species, anchored at the *Homo sapiens* tip by four trans-ancestry causal-inference chains. The clean Popperian outcome is that **Sweet Trap is real, distributed, and lineage-specific — not a Metazoan-wide synapomorphy**. Reward-fitness decoupling does not behave as a phylogenetically inherited trait, does not rest on a shared molecular substrate across distant phyla, and does not generate robust positive-selection signal distinguishable from optimiser-boundary artefacts in any tested clade after diagnostic interrogation. What it does do, on the evidence we present, is recur wherever local ecological conditions decouple proximate signals from realized fitness, on whatever reward-receptor machinery the lineage has available. Whether the moth at the streetlamp and the human at the vending machine arrived at this evolutionary attractor by parallel routes or distant inheritance is a question Paper 1 has now narrowed: distant inheritance is refuted; **parallel routes remain the testable hypothesis for Paper 2**. Our optimiser-boundary diagnostic also clarified that the Layer-4 selection signal is dominated by boundary behaviour rather than clean biological null or signal — the testable refinement for Paper 2 is therefore not just "is selection present in these clades" but "is our test powerful enough to detect it."
