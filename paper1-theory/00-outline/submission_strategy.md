# Submission Strategy — Paper 1 Theoretical Foundation

**Paper**: Sweet Trap Theory: An Axiomatic Framework for Reward–Fitness Decoupling in Evolved Decision Systems
**Authors**: Lu An, Hongyang Xi
**Status**: v1.0 draft completed, 2026-04-18
**Target level**: Kahneman-Tversky 1979 (Prospect Theory) — foundational, cite-able, named theory

---

## §1 Positioning decision

**Decision**: Paper 1 is a **theoretical foundation paper**; Paper 2 (v2.4) is the **empirical validation paper**. The pair is designed so that:

- Paper 1 can stand alone as a theoretical contribution (replicated by minimal-experimental-paradigm design).
- Paper 2 can stand alone as an empirical contribution (with Paper 1 cited as theoretical backing).
- The two together constitute a coherent research programme analogous to Kahneman-Tversky 1979 + Tversky-Kahneman 1992 (Prospect Theory + Cumulative Prospect Theory).

**Key consequence for Paper 1 submission**: we target venues that publish **formal theoretical work** with or without fresh empirical data. This narrows the space but also sharpens the pitch.

## §2 Tier 1 targets — Perspectives/Reviews slots

### §2.1 *Science* Perspectives
- Format: short (~1500 words) or Review (~5000 words)
- Usually commissioned, but solicitable via proposal
- Pro: maximum visibility; ideal for named-theory introduction
- Con: commissioning model may exclude direct submission; would need prior editorial contact

### §2.2 *Nature* Perspectives or *Nature* Review
- Format: Perspective ~3000 words; Review ~5000 words
- Similar to Science: usually commissioned
- Pro: same as Science; the Nature-brand named-theory establishment
- Con: same commissioning constraint

**Strategy for Tier 1**: write brief proposal letter (1 page) to Perspectives editors at Nature/Science, summarising the Sweet Trap framework and its four theorems, citing the 6000-word draft as backup. If either accepts a Perspective, the full paper 1 draft becomes the internal reference (`math_supplement.md`) and the Perspective is a condensed version.

**Probability estimate**: Tier 1 commissioning is a long shot (P ≈ 0.10) but high-return. Attempt in parallel with Tier 2 submissions.

## §3 Tier 2 targets — theoretical/construct paper slots

### §3.1 *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* (BBS) Target Article — **PRIMARY RECOMMENDATION**

**Fit**: BBS specifically exists for "novel, controversial ideas" in behavioural/brain sciences. Target Articles undergo open peer commentary from 15-30 researchers, creating a definitive treatment of the idea. Sweet Trap Theory is a prototypical BBS target: formal framework, cross-species, contentious (against rational-addiction tradition), empirically testable.

**Format**: 10,000 words target article + 25-30 commentaries + authors' response. Takes 18-24 months from submission to publication.

**Why primary**:
- Construct-paper ideal fit (as indicated in task brief).
- Open commentary creates large citation basis immediately — Kahneman-Tversky 1979 got to "named theory" status partly via similar commentary mechanism in Econometrica.
- BBS commentaries are indexed and citable in their own right.
- 2026 political timing: algorithmic-manipulation regulation is on the EU AI Act agenda; BBS target on the theoretical frame will be useful.

**Word-count implication**: expand draft from 6,000 to ~10,000 words, pulling from `math_supplement.md` into main text, making §3 (Theory) longer, expanding §5 (Positioning) from 500 to ~2,000 words.

**Timeline**:
- Submit Q2 2026 (after one revision cycle).
- Commentaries due Q4 2026.
- Publication Q2 2027.

### §3.2 *PNAS* (Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences)

**Fit**: PNAS accepts theoretical contributions in the "social sciences" track, including formal models. Max ~6 pages (4,500 words). Sweet Trap's cross-species claim is PNAS-appropriate.

**Pro**: faster than BBS (4-6 months); strong citation signal; broad cross-disciplinary readership matching the trans-disciplinary nature of Sweet Trap.

**Con**: 6-page limit requires compression; math supplement must be moved to SI. PNAS is less hospitable to "new named theory" framing — PNAS articles tend to be empirical. Risk: reviewer sees Paper 1 as "just theory without data" since Paper 2 is a separate submission.

**Strategy**: if BBS cycle is too slow for authors' career timeline, submit PNAS instead with tighter framing ("formal framework with immediate empirical implications supported by existing data"). Requires rewriting §4 Falsifiable Predictions to emphasise already-supported predictions P1, P3, P5.

**Timeline**: 4-6 months.

### §3.3 *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B* — theme issue

**Fit**: Phil Trans Roy Soc B regularly commissions theme issues on topics like "evolutionary mismatch", "cultural evolution", "decision-making across species". Sweet Trap Theory as a contributing paper to such a theme issue would be ideal placement.

**Pro**: theme issues are curated by guest editors; fit with other cultural-evolution and evolutionary-mismatch work; long-form theoretical papers welcome.

**Con**: requires identifying an appropriate theme issue (call-for-papers based) or proposing one (guest editor burden). Timing depends on theme-issue schedule.

**Strategy**: monitor Phil Trans B calls; submit if a 2026-2027 theme issue on "evolutionary mismatch in the Anthropocene" or "algorithmic manipulation of behaviour" opens. Otherwise not primary.

### §3.4 *Theoretical Population Biology* — niche but possible

**Fit**: most rigorous math-biology venue. Sweet Trap Theory has a mathematical-biology aspect (RST Lande-Kirkpatrick dynamics, T3 cross-species universality).

**Pro**: venue respects formal rigour; readers include Lande-generation theorists.

**Con**: narrow readership; cultural runaway and human applications sit awkwardly in a journal focused on population genetics. Paper 1's cross-disciplinary scope is too broad for ideal fit.

**Strategy**: secondary option only; or consider publishing the math-heavy version in TPB while sending conceptual version elsewhere.

### §3.5 *Perspectives on Psychological Science*

**Fit**: Association for Psychological Science's perspectives venue. Publishes theoretical psychology pieces (~8,000 words). Prospect Theory's successor pieces appear here.

**Pro**: psychology-audience reach; flexible on format.

**Con**: less cross-disciplinary than BBS; Sweet Trap's evolutionary-biology side may feel out-of-place.

### §3.6 *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* (TiCS) / *Trends in Ecology & Evolution* (TREE)

**Fit**: review-length venues (~3,500 words) for conceptual pieces. TiCS for the human/cognitive angle; TREE for the cross-species angle.

**Pro**: high citation rate; short format forces clarity.

**Con**: review format emphasises existing work; less natural for introducing a new formal theory. Better for "state of the Sweet Trap field" after the foundational paper is published elsewhere.

**Strategy**: secondary publications after Paper 1 main placement. Use as follow-ups in Q3 2027 (after BBS commentaries) to bring framework to discipline-specific audiences.

**Recommendation: BBS Target Article (§3.1)**.

Justification:
1. Prototypical fit for named-theory construct paper.
2. 10,000-word format accommodates full formal content without forcing SI relegation.

## §7 Risk assessment

**Risk 1**: Reviewers ask "why a new theory if Mismatch + Ecological Trap exist?"
- **Response**: §5 Positioning makes formal containment explicit; Mismatch has no decision rule, Eco Trap is habitat-restricted. Sweet Trap is a strict extension, not relabeling. T2 is the novel result.

**Risk 2**: Reviewers (especially economists) object to *w*_max < 1/2 as ad hoc.
- **Response**: §A of `weak_joints_resolution.md` treats this explicitly. *w*_max < 1/2 is the minimum requirement for the concept to make sense; empirical estimates cluster at 0.15–0.35. Tier A/B stratification handles boundary populations.

**Risk 3**: Reviewers (biologists) object to treating cultural runaway as analogous to Fisher runaway.
- **Response**: Lemma L4 + cultural-evolution literature (Cavalli-Sforza-Feldman 1981; Boyd-Richerson 1985; Henrich-McElreath 2003) establish the selection-gradient substitution as standard, not novel. This is explicitly addressed in `weak_joints_resolution.md` §2.

**Risk 4**: Reviewers ask for more empirical data in Paper 1.
- **Response**: Paper 1 cites Paper 2 v2.4 as empirical companion. Five predictions in §4 are declared with current empirical status. Minimal experimental paradigm (§6 Discussion) is pre-registered for further falsification. Paper 1 is explicitly framed as theoretical foundation.
